Abstract

We present a categorical framework for the planner engine of an AI operating system, establishing it as the highest-level orchestration primitive analogous to systemd/init in classical operating systems. The planner is formalized as a natural transformation \(\eta: F \Rightarrow G\) between the task functor \(F\), mapping jobs to requirement specifications, and the capability functor \(G\), mapping agents to offered capabilities. We introduce an order book structure modeled as a profunctor \(P: \mathcal{A}^{\mathrm{op}} \times \mathcal{T} \to \mathbf{Set}\), through which agents (sellers) and clients (buyers) interact via a bidding protocol. Market clearing is characterized as a colimit in the transaction category, and the escrow mechanism is shown to satisfy the monad laws, guaranteeing financial consistency of credit operations. Job decomposition into directed acyclic graphs with topological execution ordering is proven to be functorial, preserving job semantics under decomposition. We validate the framework through an Elixir/OTP implementation featuring GenServer-based order books, Mnesia-backed escrow transactions, and supervision-tree-based task decomposition, drawing on the architecture of a production AI agent marketplace.

Keywords: AI planning, natural transformation, order book, profunctor, escrow monad, market microstructure, task decomposition, reputation systems, Elixir/OTP, agent marketplace

1. Introduction

Every operating system has a process that runs first and orchestrates everything else. In Unix, this is init (PID 1). In modern Linux, systemd manages service dependencies, socket activation, and lifecycle transitions. In Kubernetes, the scheduler assigns pods to nodes based on resource requests, affinity rules, and priority classes. These systems solve the same fundamental problem: given a set of work items and a set of executors, determine who does what, when, and how.

An AI operating system faces this problem at a higher level of abstraction. The “processes” are not deterministic programs but stochastic agents with heterogeneous capabilities, varying reliability, and economic incentives. The “scheduler” must not only assign tasks but must negotiate prices, hold funds in escrow, verify quality, and update reputations. In short, the planner engine of an AI OS is simultaneously:

  1. A task decomposer that breaks complex jobs into dependency graphs of subtasks;
  2. A marketplace where agents bid on work and clients select providers;
  3. A financial system managing credits, escrow, and revenue distribution;
  4. A reputation engine that scores quality and prevents gaming.

This paper provides a unified categorical framework for all four aspects. The central insight is that planning is a natural transformation between two functors: one that maps jobs to their requirements, and another that maps agents to their capabilities. The naturality condition ensures that planning decisions are consistent—changing the decomposition of a job and then matching is the same as matching and then decomposing the matched components.

The marketplace aspect is captured by an order book modeled as a profunctor. Just as in financial markets, buy orders (task requests) and sell orders (agent proposals) accumulate until a matching occurs. The escrow mechanism, which holds client credits until work is verified, satisfies the monad laws, providing algebraic guarantees of financial consistency. Market clearing—the moment when a proposal is accepted and a contract is formed—is characterized as a colimit in the transaction category.

We ground every abstraction in a concrete system: the AgentHero marketplace, where jobs follow the lifecycle draft → open → in_progress → review/completed/cancelled/disputed, operators submit proposals with execution plans and confidence scores, and atomic credit operations via Supabase RPC prevent race conditions. We provide a complete Elixir/OTP implementation demonstrating that the categorical structure maps directly to production-quality OTP patterns.

2. Background

2.1 Process Orchestration in Classical Operating Systems

The evolution of process orchestration follows a clear trajectory of increasing abstraction:

Unix init

The original init process reads /etc/inittab and spawns services in a fixed order determined by runlevels. Dependencies are implicit in the ordering of startup scripts. This approach is inherently sequential and fragile—a slow service blocks all subsequent ones.

systemd

Poettering and Sievers' systemd introduced a declarative dependency graph. Each unit file specifies Requires, After, Wants, and Conflicts relationships. The dependency graph is topologically sorted, and independent services start in parallel. Socket activation allows services to start on-demand.

Kubernetes

The Kubernetes scheduler operates at the cluster level. It maintains a queue of unscheduled pods and iterates through a pipeline of predicates (hard constraints: resource availability, node affinity, taints/tolerations) and priorities (soft preferences: load balancing, data locality). Critically, Kubernetes separates the desired state (declared in manifests) from the actual state (observed by controllers), with reconciliation loops driving convergence.

From orchestration to markets

Each of these systems assumes a benevolent, centralized scheduler that allocates resources optimally. In an AI agent ecosystem, this assumption breaks down. Agents are autonomous, self-interested entities. They may misrepresent capabilities, deliver substandard work, or collude. The planner must therefore incorporate market mechanisms—pricing, bidding, escrow, reputation—that align individual incentives with system-wide efficiency.

2.2 Market Microstructure

Market microstructure theory studies how trading mechanisms affect price formation, liquidity, and information aggregation.

Order books

A limit order book (LOB) maintains buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks), each specifying a price and quantity. Orders are matched when a bid price meets or exceeds an ask price. The spread between the best bid and best ask reflects liquidity and information asymmetry.

Matching engines

Matching follows priority rules: price-time priority (best price first, then earliest arrival) is standard. Pro-rata matching allocates proportionally at the same price level. In our setting, “price” is the credit cost and “quantity” is a unit of agent work.

Mechanism design

The revelation principle states that any mechanism can be replicated by a direct revelation mechanism where truthful reporting is incentive-compatible. The Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) mechanism achieves efficiency and truthfulness for combinatorial auctions. We draw on these results to design anti-gaming mechanisms for agent reputation.

2.3 Category Theory Preliminaries

Definition 2.1 (Profunctor)

A profunctor \(P: \mathbf{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \times \mathbf{D} \to \mathbf{Set}\) is a functor from the product of the opposite of \(\mathbf{C}\) with \(\mathbf{D}\) to the category of sets. It generalizes the notion of a relation between categories. For objects \(c \in \mathbf{C}\) and \(d \in \mathbf{D}\), \(P(c, d)\) is the set of “heteromorphisms” from \(c\) to \(d\).

Definition 2.2 (Monad)

A monad on a category \(\mathbf{C}\) is a triple \((T, \mu, \eta)\) where \(T: \mathbf{C} \to \mathbf{C}\) is an endofunctor, \(\eta: \mathrm{id}_{\mathbf{C}} \Rightarrow T\) is the unit, and \(\mu: T^2 \Rightarrow T\) is the multiplication, satisfying:

$$\mu \circ T\mu = \mu \circ \mu T \quad \text{(associativity)}$$ $$\mu \circ T\eta = \mu \circ \eta T = \mathrm{id}_T \quad \text{(unit laws)}$$
Definition 2.3 (Colimit)

Given a diagram \(D: \mathbf{J} \to \mathbf{C}\), a colimit of \(D\) is an object \(\mathrm{colim}\, D\) in \(\mathbf{C}\) together with a cocone \(\{\iota_j: D(j) \to \mathrm{colim}\, D\}_{j \in \mathbf{J}}\) that is universal: for any other cocone \(\{\phi_j: D(j) \to X\}_{j \in \mathbf{J}}\), there exists a unique morphism \(u: \mathrm{colim}\, D \to X\) with \(u \circ \iota_j = \phi_j\) for all \(j\).

3. Market Model — Formal Framework

We now construct the categorical framework for the agent marketplace. The fundamental insight is that a marketplace is a category whose objects are economic participants and whose morphisms are transactions between them.

3.1 The Market Category

Definition 3.1 (Market Category)

The market category \(\mathcal{M}\) is defined as follows:

  • Objects: Economic participants, partitioned into clients \(\mathcal{B}\) (buyers), operators \(\mathcal{S}\) (sellers/agents), and the platform \(\pi\). Thus \(\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{M}) = \mathcal{B} \sqcup \mathcal{S} \sqcup \{\pi\}\).
  • Morphisms: A morphism \(f: a \to b\) is a transaction transferring credits from participant \(a\) to participant \(b\). Each morphism carries an amount \(\mathrm{amt}(f) \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\), a timestamp \(\mathrm{time}(f) \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\), and a transaction type \(\mathrm{type}(f) \in \{\texttt{escrow\_hold}, \texttt{escrow\_release}, \texttt{payment}, \texttt{refund}, \texttt{fee}\}\).
  • Composition: Sequential transactions compose: if \(f: a \to b\) and \(g: b \to c\), then \(g \circ f: a \to c\) with \(\mathrm{amt}(g \circ f) = \mathrm{amt}(g)\).
  • Identity: The identity \(\mathrm{id}_a: a \to a\) is the zero-transfer transaction.
Remark

The market category is enriched over \((\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, +, 0)\): the hom-sets carry a monoidal structure given by addition of transaction amounts. This enrichment captures the financial flow structure.

3.2 The Order Book as a Profunctor

Definition 3.2 (Agent and Task Categories)

Define the agent category \(\mathcal{A}\) and the task category \(\mathcal{T}\) as follows:

  • \(\mathcal{A}\): Objects are agents (operators). A morphism \(\alpha \to \alpha'\) exists when agent \(\alpha'\) can simulate or subsume the capabilities of \(\alpha\) (capability ordering).
  • \(\mathcal{T}\): Objects are tasks (jobs or subtasks). A morphism \(\tau \to \tau'\) exists when task \(\tau\) is a prerequisite of \(\tau'\) (dependency ordering).
Definition 3.3 (Order Book Profunctor)

The order book is a profunctor

$$P: \mathcal{A}^{\mathrm{op}} \times \mathcal{T} \to \mathbf{Set}$$

where \(P(\alpha, \tau)\) is the set of proposals (sell orders) from agent \(\alpha\) for task \(\tau\). Each proposal \(p \in P(\alpha, \tau)\) consists of:

$$p = (\texttt{execution\_plan}, \texttt{estimated\_credits}, \texttt{estimated\_duration}, \texttt{confidence\_score})$$

The profunctor action is:

  • Contravariant in \(\mathcal{A}\): If \(\alpha' \to \alpha\) (i.e., \(\alpha\) subsumes \(\alpha'\)), then any proposal from \(\alpha'\) can be lifted to a proposal from \(\alpha\): \(P(\alpha', \tau) \to P(\alpha, \tau)\).
  • Covariant in \(\mathcal{T}\): If \(\tau \to \tau'\) (i.e., \(\tau\) is a prerequisite of \(\tau'\)), then completing \(\tau\) enables proposals for \(\tau'\): there is an induced map \(P(\alpha, \tau) \to P(\alpha, \tau')\).
Definition 3.4 (Demand Profunctor)

The demand profunctor is

$$Q: \mathcal{B}^{\mathrm{op}} \times \mathcal{T} \to \mathbf{Set}$$

where \(Q(b, \tau)\) is the set of job postings by client \(b\) for task \(\tau\), each specifying a budget ceiling, required capabilities, and deadline.

3.3 Market Clearing as a Colimit

Definition 3.5 (Transaction Diagram)

Given a task \(\tau\) with demand \(q \in Q(b, \tau)\) and proposals \(\{p_i \in P(\alpha_i, \tau)\}_{i \in I}\), the transaction diagram is the functor \(D: \mathbf{J} \to \mathcal{M}\) where \(\mathbf{J}\) is the index category with objects \(\{b, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n, \pi\}\) and morphisms representing potential transactions, and \(D\) maps each object to the corresponding participant in \(\mathcal{M}\).

Theorem 3.1 (Market Clearing as Colimit)

Let \(D: \mathbf{J} \to \mathcal{M}\) be a transaction diagram for task \(\tau\). The market clearing event—where one proposal \(p_k\) is accepted and all others \(\{p_i\}_{i \neq k}\) are rejected—is the colimit \(\mathrm{colim}\, D\) in \(\mathcal{M}\). The universal cocone consists of:

  1. The escrow morphism \(e: b \to \pi\) with \(\mathrm{amt}(e) = \texttt{estimated\_credits}(p_k)\);
  2. The contract formation \(c: \pi \to \alpha_k\) (pending work completion);
  3. Rejection morphisms \(r_i: \alpha_i \to \alpha_i\) (identity, no transaction) for \(i \neq k\).
Proof. We verify the universal property. Let \(\{e, c, r_i\}\) be the proposed cocone. For any other cocone \(\{\phi_j\}_{j \in \mathbf{J}}\) over the same diagram, the unique mediating morphism \(u: \mathrm{colim}\, D \to X\) is determined by the contract terms: the escrow amount uniquely determines the payment flow, and the accepted proposal uniquely determines the executor. The rejection morphisms are identities, so they factor trivially. The uniqueness of \(u\) follows from the budget ceiling constraint—only one allocation of the client's credits is consistent with accepting exactly one proposal. To show existence, observe that accepting proposal \(p_k\) induces \(\texttt{holdEscrow}(b, \texttt{estimated\_credits}(p_k), \texttt{contract\_id})\), which atomically decrements the client's balance and creates an escrow record. This atomic operation, implemented via Supabase RPC, ensures that the colimit exists and is unique (no double-spending).
Corollary 3.2

In the colimit construction, accepting proposal \(p_k\) necessarily rejects all other proposals \(\{p_i\}_{i \neq k}\). This is the universal property at work: the cocone maps are uniquely determined.

This matches the AgentHero implementation exactly: when a client accepts one proposal, all others are auto-rejected, and a contract is created with escrowed credits.

3.4 The Escrow Monad

Definition 3.6 (Credit Category)

The credit category \(\mathcal{C}\) has:

  • Objects: credit balances \((b, n)\) where \(b\) is a participant and \(n \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\) is the balance;
  • Morphisms: balance transitions \((b, n) \to (b, m)\) representing credit/debit operations.
Definition 3.7 (Escrow Monad)

The escrow monad \(E: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}\) is the endofunctor defined by:

$$E(b, n) = (b, n - h, h) \quad \text{where } h \text{ is the held amount}$$ $$\eta_{(b,n)}: (b, n) \to E(b, n) = (b, n, 0) \quad \text{(unit: no hold)}$$ $$\mu_{(b,n,h)}: E(E(b, n)) \to E(b, n) \quad \text{(join: flatten nested holds)}$$

The monadic operations correspond to:

  • Unit (\(\eta\)): Initialize escrow with zero hold. return in Haskell.
  • Bind (\(\gg\!\!=\)): Sequence escrow operations. Holding credits for contract \(c_1\) and then for \(c_2\) composes into a single escrow state.
  • Join (\(\mu\)): Flatten \(E(E(b, n))\) to \(E(b, n)\)—settle an escrow-within-escrow (e.g., subcontracting).

4. Planning as Natural Transformation

We now formalize the planner as the central natural transformation connecting what needs to be done with who can do it.

4.1 The Task Functor

Definition 4.1 (Requirement Category)

The requirement category \(\mathcal{R}\) has:

  • Objects: requirement specifications \(r = (\texttt{capabilities}, \texttt{constraints}, \texttt{budget}, \texttt{deadline})\);
  • Morphisms: requirement refinements \(r \to r'\) where \(r'\) is a stricter specification.
Definition 4.2 (Task Functor)

The task functor \(F: \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{R}\) maps each task to its requirement specification:

$$F(\tau) = (\texttt{required\_capabilities}(\tau), \texttt{constraints}(\tau), \texttt{budget}(\tau), \texttt{deadline}(\tau))$$

For a morphism \(\tau \to \tau'\) (dependency), \(F\) maps it to the induced requirement refinement.

4.2 The Capability Functor

Definition 4.3 (Capability Category)

The capability category \(\mathcal{K}\) has:

  • Objects: capability profiles \(k = (\texttt{skills}, \texttt{capacity}, \texttt{pricing}, \texttt{reputation})\);
  • Morphisms: capability enhancements \(k \to k'\) where \(k'\) has additional skills or capacity.
Definition 4.4 (Capability Functor)

The capability functor \(G: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{K}\) maps each agent to its capability profile:

$$G(\alpha) = (\texttt{skills}(\alpha), \texttt{capacity}(\alpha), \texttt{pricing\_model}(\alpha), \texttt{reputation}(\alpha))$$

where \(\texttt{pricing\_model} \in \{\texttt{per\_task}, \texttt{hourly}, \texttt{per\_token}\}\).

4.3 The Planner Natural Transformation

Definition 4.5 (Planner)

The planner is a natural transformation

$$\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M$$

where \(M: \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{A}\) is the matching functor (assigning agents to tasks). For each task \(\tau\), the component \(\eta_\tau: F(\tau) \to G(M(\tau))\) witnesses that the assigned agent's capabilities satisfy the task's requirements.

The naturality condition gives the following commutative diagram. For every morphism \(f: \tau \to \tau'\) in \(\mathcal{T}\) (a dependency relation):

F(τ) ——η_τ——→ G(M(τ))
  |                      |
  | F(f)                 | G(M(f))
  ↓                      ↓
F(τ') ——η_τ'——→ G(M(τ'))
Theorem 4.1 (Planning Consistency)

Let \(\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M\) be the planner natural transformation. The naturality condition

$$G(M(f)) \circ \eta_\tau = \eta_{\tau'} \circ F(f)$$

for every dependency \(f: \tau \to \tau'\) guarantees planning consistency: the following two procedures yield the same result:

  1. Refine the requirements of \(\tau\) to those of \(\tau'\) (via \(F(f)\)), then match \(\tau'\) to an agent (via \(\eta_{\tau'}\)).
  2. Match \(\tau\) to an agent (via \(\eta_\tau\)), then enhance the agent's capability commitment from that of \(\tau\) to that of \(\tau'\) (via \(G(M(f))\)).
Proof. This is precisely the naturality square. The condition states that the diagram commutes, i.e., the two paths from \(F(\tau)\) to \(G(M(\tau'))\) are equal. Concretely, this means that planning decisions are stable under requirement refinement. If task \(\tau\) is a prerequisite of \(\tau'\), then the agent assigned to handle the chain \(\tau \to \tau'\) must be capable of handling the refined requirements. This prevents the situation where an agent is assigned to \(\tau\) but cannot handle the downstream task \(\tau'\) that depends on \(\tau\)'s output.
Corollary 4.2 (Optimal Matching)

The optimal matching functor \(M^*\) minimizes the cost functional

$$M^* = \arg\min_{M} \sum_{\tau \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{T})} \texttt{cost}(\eta_\tau)$$

subject to the naturality constraint \(G(M(f)) \circ \eta_\tau = \eta_{\tau'} \circ F(f)\) for all dependencies \(f\), where:

$$\texttt{cost}(\eta_\tau) = \frac{\texttt{estimated\_credits}(M(\tau), \tau)}{1 + \texttt{confidence\_score}(M(\tau), \tau) \cdot \texttt{reputation}(M(\tau))}$$
Proof. The cost functional is well-defined on the space of natural transformations \(\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M\) because the naturality constraint restricts \(M\) to those matchings that respect task dependencies. The minimum exists because the set of agents is finite, the tasks are finite, and the cost functional is bounded below by zero.

4.4 The Matching Process

In the AgentHero system, matching proceeds through the following stages:

  1. Job posting (draft → open): A client creates a job with requirements \(F(\tau)\). This defines the left-hand side of the naturality square.
  2. Proposal submission: Operators inspect \(F(\tau)\) and submit proposals \(p \in P(\alpha, \tau)\). Each proposal is a candidate component \(\eta_\tau^{(\alpha)}: F(\tau) \to G(\alpha)\).
  3. Proposal evaluation: The client evaluates proposals. This is a selection over the fiber \(P(-, \tau) = \coprod_\alpha P(\alpha, \tau)\).
  4. Acceptance (open → in_progress): The client accepts proposal \(p_k\) from agent \(\alpha_k\). This selects the component \(\eta_\tau = \eta_\tau^{(\alpha_k)}\) and defines \(M(\tau) = \alpha_k\). All other proposals are auto-rejected (Corollary 3.2).
  5. Contract creation: The acceptance triggers holdEscrow(b, rate_credits, contract_id), applying the escrow monad unit.
  6. Execution and review (in_progress → review): The agent executes the task. Upon completion, the work enters review.
  7. Settlement (review → completed): Approval releases escrow via the monad join, distributing credits: 70% operator, 15% platform, 15% LLM reserve.

5. Job Decomposition

Complex jobs must be broken into manageable subtasks. The AgentHero orchestrator performs this decomposition, producing a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of subtasks with dependency edges.

5.1 The Decomposition Functor

Definition 5.1 (DAG Category)

Let \(\mathbf{DAG}\) be the category whose objects are directed acyclic graphs \((V, E)\) with \(V\) a finite set of vertices (subtasks) and \(E \subseteq V \times V\) the edge set (dependencies). A morphism \(\phi: (V, E) \to (V', E')\) is a graph homomorphism preserving the partial order.

Definition 5.2 (Decomposition Functor)

The decomposition functor \(\Delta: \mathcal{T} \to \mathbf{DAG}\) maps each task \(\tau\) to its decomposition graph \(\Delta(\tau) = (V_\tau, E_\tau)\), where:

  • \(V_\tau = \{\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n\}\) are the subtasks;
  • \((\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \in E_\tau\) iff \(\sigma_i\) must complete before \(\sigma_j\) begins;
  • For a dependency \(f: \tau \to \tau'\), the morphism \(\Delta(f): \Delta(\tau) \to \Delta(\tau')\) maps terminal subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau)\) to initial subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau')\).
Proposition 5.1 (Decomposition Preserves Job Semantics)

The decomposition functor \(\Delta\) is faithful: distinct tasks have distinct decompositions, and the dependency structure is preserved. Formally:

  1. \(\Delta\) preserves identities: \(\Delta(\mathrm{id}_\tau) = \mathrm{id}_{\Delta(\tau)}\).
  2. \(\Delta\) preserves composition: \(\Delta(g \circ f) = \Delta(g) \circ \Delta(f)\) for composable dependencies \(f, g\).

Consequently, executing the decomposed subtasks in any topological order that respects \(E_\tau\) is equivalent to executing the original task \(\tau\).

Proof. Preservation of identities: the identity dependency \(\mathrm{id}_\tau: \tau \to \tau\) maps to the identity graph homomorphism on \(\Delta(\tau)\). Preservation of composition: let \(f: \tau_1 \to \tau_2\) and \(g: \tau_2 \to \tau_3\). Then \(\Delta(f)\) connects terminal subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_1)\) to initial subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_2)\), and \(\Delta(g)\) connects terminal subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_2)\) to initial subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_3)\). The composition \(\Delta(g) \circ \Delta(f)\) is the graph homomorphism that connects terminal subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_1)\) through \(\Delta(\tau_2)\) to initial subtasks of \(\Delta(\tau_3)\), which is exactly \(\Delta(g \circ f)\). Faithfulness: distinct dependencies induce distinct graph homomorphisms because the subtask structure carries strictly more information than the coarse dependency.

5.2 Topological Sort for Execution Ordering

Given a DAG \(\Delta(\tau) = (V_\tau, E_\tau)\), we compute a topological ordering \(\sigma_{i_1}, \sigma_{i_2}, \ldots, \sigma_{i_n}\) such that \((\sigma_{i_j}, \sigma_{i_k}) \in E_\tau \implies j < k\).

Algorithm 1: Topological Sort with Parallel Barrier Detection
Input: DAG (V, E) with |V| = n
Output: Execution schedule S = [L_1, L_2, ..., L_m] where each L_i is a parallel level

1. Compute in-degrees: for all v in V, deg^-(v) = |{u : (u, v) in E}|
2. L_0 := {v in V : deg^-(v) = 0}    // sources: no dependencies
3. S := [L_0]
4. i := 0
5. while union(L_0..L_i) != V do
6.     L_{i+1} := {}
7.     for each v in L_i do
8.         for each (v, w) in E do
9.             deg^-(w) := deg^-(w) - 1
10.            if deg^-(w) = 0 then
11.                L_{i+1} := L_{i+1} union {w}
12.    S := S ++ [L_{i+1}]
13.    i := i + 1
14. return S

Each level \(L_i\) consists of subtasks that can execute in parallel—their dependencies are all satisfied by the completion of levels \(L_0, \ldots, L_{i-1}\). The transition from \(L_i\) to \(L_{i+1}\) is a barrier synchronization.

Proposition 5.2 (Optimality of Level-Based Parallelism)

The number of levels \(m\) in Algorithm 1 equals the length of the longest path in the DAG. This is the theoretical minimum number of sequential steps required, and is therefore optimal for parallel execution.

Proof. Each level contains all vertices whose longest incoming path has the same length. The critical path (longest path from any source to any sink) determines \(m\). No schedule can execute in fewer than \(m\) steps, because the critical path tasks are sequentially dependent.

5.3 Integration with Inngest Durable Execution

In the AgentHero implementation, each subtask execution is wrapped in an Inngest function, providing event-driven triggers, automatic retries with exponential backoff, idempotency, and observability. The categorical interpretation is a functor from the DAG category to the category of Inngest functions:

$$\Phi: \mathbf{DAG} \to \mathbf{Inngest}$$

where \(\mathbf{Inngest}\) is the category of event-driven durable functions with composition given by event chaining.

6. Financial Mechanics

6.1 The Escrow Monad in Detail

Definition 6.1 (Escrow Endofunctor)

Define \(E: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}\) on objects by

$$E(b, n) = (b, n_{\text{available}}, n_{\text{held}})$$

where \(n = n_{\text{available}} + n_{\text{held}}\), and on morphisms by

$$E(f: (b,n) \to (b,m)) = (f_{\text{avail}}: n_{\text{available}} \to m_{\text{available}},\; f_{\text{held}}: n_{\text{held}} \to m_{\text{held}})$$

preserving the invariant \(n_{\text{available}} + n_{\text{held}} = m_{\text{available}} + m_{\text{held}}\) (credits are conserved).

Definition 6.2 (Escrow Unit and Multiplication)

The unit \(\eta: \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}} \Rightarrow E\) is:

$$\eta_{(b,n)}: (b, n) \mapsto (b, n, 0) \quad \text{(no credits held)}$$

The multiplication \(\mu: E^2 \Rightarrow E\) flattens nested escrow:

$$\mu_{(b,n)}: E(E(b, n)) = (b, n_{\text{avail}}, (n_{\text{held\_outer}}, n_{\text{held\_inner}})) \mapsto (b, n_{\text{avail}}, n_{\text{held\_outer}} + n_{\text{held\_inner}})$$

This corresponds to settling a subcontract escrow into the parent escrow.

Theorem 6.1 (Escrow Monad Laws)

The triple \((E, \eta, \mu)\) satisfies the monad laws:

  1. Left unit: \(\mu \circ \eta E = \mathrm{id}_E\)
  2. Right unit: \(\mu \circ E\eta = \mathrm{id}_E\)
  3. Associativity: \(\mu \circ \mu E = \mu \circ E\mu\)
Proof. Left unit. Starting from \(E(b,n) = (b, n_a, n_h)\): $$(\mu \circ \eta E)(b, n_a, n_h) = \mu(\eta(b, n_a, n_h)) = \mu(b, (n_a, n_h), 0) = (b, n_a, n_h + 0) = (b, n_a, n_h) = \mathrm{id}_E(b, n_a, n_h)$$ Right unit. Starting from \(E(b,n) = (b, n_a, n_h)\): $$(\mu \circ E\eta)(b, n_a, n_h) = \mu(E(\eta)(b, n_a, n_h)) = \mu(b, n_a, (n_h, 0)) = (b, n_a, n_h + 0) = \mathrm{id}_E(b, n_a, n_h)$$ Associativity. Starting from \(E^3(b,n)\) with three levels of holding \((h_1, h_2, h_3)\): $$(\mu \circ \mu E)(b, n_a, (h_1, (h_2, h_3))) = \mu(b, n_a, (h_1, h_2 + h_3)) = (b, n_a, h_1 + h_2 + h_3)$$ $$(\mu \circ E\mu)(b, n_a, (h_1, (h_2, h_3))) = \mu(b, n_a, (h_1 + h_2, h_3)) = (b, n_a, h_1 + h_2 + h_3)$$ Both paths yield the same result, since addition is associative.

6.2 Revenue Split as Natural Transformation

Definition 6.3 (Revenue Split)

The revenue split is a natural transformation \(\rho: E \Rightarrow D\) where \(D: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}^3\) is the distribution functor mapping a single credit pool to a triple:

$$D(n_{\text{held}}) = (0.70 \cdot n_{\text{held}},\; 0.15 \cdot n_{\text{held}},\; 0.15 \cdot n_{\text{held}})$$

corresponding to (operator payment, platform fee, LLM reserve).

The three pricing models affect how \(n_{\text{held}}\) is computed:

$$n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{per\_task}} = \texttt{fixed\_price}$$ $$n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{hourly}} = \texttt{rate\_credits} \times \lceil \texttt{hours\_worked} \rceil$$ $$n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{per\_token}} = \texttt{rate\_per\_token} \times \texttt{tokens\_consumed}$$

In all cases, \(n_{\text{held}} \leq \texttt{budget\_ceiling}\) to prevent overspend.

6.3 Atomic Operations and Race Condition Prevention

Definition 6.4 (Atomic Credit Operations)

An atomic credit operation is a morphism in \(\mathcal{C}\) that is implemented via a database-level serializable transaction. In the AgentHero system, this is a Supabase RPC call:

Supabase RPC for atomic escrow
adminClient.rpc('hold_escrow', {
  p_client_id: client_id,
  p_amount: rate_credits,
  p_contract_id: contract_id
})

The RPC function executes within a PostgreSQL transaction with SERIALIZABLE isolation, ensuring: (1) the balance check and deduction are atomic (no TOCTOU race); (2) negative balances are prevented by a CHECK(balance >= 0) constraint; (3) concurrent operations are serialized by the database.

Theorem 6.2 (Financial Consistency)

Under the escrow monad with atomic credit operations, the following invariants hold:

  1. Conservation: The total credits in the system (available + held + distributed) is constant.
  2. Non-negativity: No participant's available balance is ever negative.
  3. Escrow completeness: Every held amount is eventually either released (completed) or refunded (cancelled/disputed).
Proof. Conservation follows from the monad laws: \(\eta\) preserves totals (holds move credits from available to held, not creating or destroying them), and \(\mu\) flattens without changing totals. Non-negativity is enforced by the database constraint CHECK(balance >= 0). The atomic RPC rolls back if this constraint would be violated. Escrow completeness follows from the job lifecycle being a finite state machine with terminal states \(\{\texttt{completed}, \texttt{cancelled}, \texttt{disputed}\}\). Each terminal state triggers either release or refund. With Inngest durable execution, liveness is guaranteed: the event-driven pipeline will eventually reach a terminal state (possibly via timeout).

7. Reputation and Trust

7.1 Six-Dimensional Quality Scoring

Definition 7.1 (Quality Vector)

The quality vector for a completed job is \(\mathbf{q} \in [0, 1]^6\) with components:

$$\mathbf{q} = (q_{\text{accuracy}},\; q_{\text{completeness}},\; q_{\text{timeliness}},\; q_{\text{communication}},\; q_{\text{efficiency}},\; q_{\text{innovation}})$$

where:

  • \(q_{\text{accuracy}}\): correctness of deliverables against requirements;
  • \(q_{\text{completeness}}\): fraction of requirements addressed;
  • \(q_{\text{timeliness}}\): \(\max(0, 1 - \frac{\text{actual\_duration} - \text{estimated\_duration}}{\text{deadline}})\);
  • \(q_{\text{communication}}\): responsiveness and clarity (client-rated);
  • \(q_{\text{efficiency}}\): \(1 - \frac{\text{actual\_credits} - \text{estimated\_credits}}{\text{budget\_ceiling}}\);
  • \(q_{\text{innovation}}\): exceeding requirements or novel approaches (evaluator-scored).
Definition 7.2 (Reputation Score)

The reputation score of agent \(\alpha\) after \(N\) completed jobs is the exponentially-weighted inner product:

$$R(\alpha) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \lambda^{N-i} \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{q}_i \rangle}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \lambda^{N-i}}$$

where \(\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^6_{\geq 0}\) with \(\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 = 1\) is a weight vector (default: uniform \(\mathbf{w} = (\frac{1}{6}, \ldots, \frac{1}{6})\)), and \(\lambda \in (0, 1)\) is the decay factor (default: \(\lambda = 0.95\)), giving more weight to recent performance.

7.2 Reputation as a Functor

Definition 7.3 (Reputation Functor)

The reputation functor \(\mathcal{R}: \mathcal{A} \to [0, 1]\) maps each agent to its reputation score and each capability enhancement morphism \(\alpha \to \alpha'\) to the induced reputation update. The codomain \([0,1]\) is viewed as a poset category.

Proposition 7.1 (Reputation Convergence)

Under honest participation (i.e., quality vectors \(\mathbf{q}_i\) are drawn i.i.d. from a distribution with mean \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and finite variance), the reputation score converges:

$$R(\alpha) \xrightarrow{N \to \infty} \langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle$$

almost surely.

Proof. The exponentially-weighted average is a consistent estimator of \(\langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle\). Writing \(X_i = \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{q}_i \rangle\), we have \(\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle\) and $$R(\alpha) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i}} = \frac{1 - \lambda}{1 - \lambda^N} \sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i$$ As \(N \to \infty\), \(\frac{1-\lambda}{1-\lambda^N} \to (1-\lambda)\) and \(\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i\) is a geometrically-weighted sum. By the strong law of large numbers for weighted averages, convergence holds a.s. provided \(\mathrm{Var}(X_i) < \infty\), which is guaranteed since \(X_i \in [0,1]\).

7.3 Anti-Gaming Mechanisms

Definition 7.4 (Gaming Detection)

An agent \(\alpha\) is flagged for suspected gaming if any of the following anomaly detectors fire:

  1. Self-dealing: \(\alpha\) and the client share a wallet address or IP cluster.
  2. Score inflation: The quality variance \(\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_N) < \epsilon\) for suspiciously many jobs.
  3. Rapid cycling: Jobs complete in less than \(\delta_{\min}\) time with perfect scores.
  4. Collusion ring: A clique of agents and clients who only transact with each other, detected via graph analysis.
  5. Reputation laundering: Creating a new agent identity after accumulating negative reputation.
Definition 7.5 (Trust Score)

The trust score is:

$$T(\alpha) = R(\alpha) \cdot (1 - \gamma(\alpha)) \cdot \min\left(1, \frac{N(\alpha)}{N_{\min}}\right)$$

where \(\gamma(\alpha) \in [0, 1]\) is the gaming suspicion level, and the third factor is a “seasoning” term requiring a minimum number of jobs \(N_{\min}\) before full trust is granted.

8.2 Order Book GenServer

Listing 2: Order book GenServer (simplified)
defmodule PlannerEngine.OrderBook do
  use GenServer

  # Submit proposal (sell order)
  def submit_proposal(proposal) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:submit_proposal, proposal})
  end

  # Post demand (buy order)
  def post_demand(demand) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:post_demand, demand})
  end

  def handle_call({:submit_proposal, proposal}, _from, state) do
    task_id = proposal.task_id
    proposals = Map.update(
      state.proposals, task_id, [proposal],
      &insert_by_priority(&1, proposal)
    )
    new_state = %{state | proposals: proposals}
    {:reply, :ok, maybe_match(new_state, task_id)}
  end

  # Price-time priority insertion
  defp insert_by_priority(existing, new) do
    Enum.sort_by([new | existing], fn p ->
      {p.estimated_credits, p.timestamp}
    end)
  end

  # Match when demand meets supply
  defp maybe_match(state, task_id) do
    with demands when demands != [] <-
           Map.get(state.demands, task_id, []),
         [best | _rest] <-
           Map.get(state.proposals, task_id, []),
         true <- best.estimated_credits <=
                   hd(demands).budget_ceiling do
      execute_match(state, task_id, hd(demands), best)
    else
      _ -> state
    end
  end
end

8.3 Escrow GenServer with Mnesia

Listing 3: Escrow monad implementation
defmodule PlannerEngine.Escrow do
  use GenServer

  # Monad unit: initialize escrow (hold credits)
  def hold(client_id, amount, contract_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__,
      {:hold, client_id, amount, contract_id})
  end

  # Monad bind: sequence escrow operations
  def bind(escrow_id, f) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:bind, escrow_id, f})
  end

  # Monad join: settle (release or refund)
  def settle(escrow_id, action) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__,
      {:settle, escrow_id, action})
  end

  def handle_call({:hold, client_id, amount, cid},
                  _from, state) do
    result = :mnesia.transaction(fn ->
      case :mnesia.read(:balances, client_id) do
        [{:balances, ^client_id, balance}]
          when balance >= amount ->
          :mnesia.write({:balances, client_id,
                         balance - amount})
          escrow_id = generate_id()
          :mnesia.write({:escrows, escrow_id,
            client_id, amount, cid, :held})
          escrow_id
        _ ->
          :mnesia.abort(:insufficient_funds)
      end
    end)

    case result do
      {:atomic, eid} -> {:reply, {:ok, eid}, state}
      {:aborted, reason} ->
        {:reply, {:error, reason}, state}
    end
  end
end

8.4 Task Decomposition with Supervision Trees

Listing 4: DAG-based task decomposition
defmodule PlannerEngine.Decomposer do
  def decompose(task) do
    subtasks = analyze_and_split(task)
    edges = infer_dependencies(subtasks)

    dag = %{
      task_id: task.id,
      subtasks: Map.new(subtasks, &{&1.id, &1}),
      edges: edges
    }

    case validate_dag(dag) do
      :ok -> {:ok, dag}
      {:error, :cycle} -> {:error, :cyclic_dependency}
    end
  end

  def topological_sort(dag) do
    in_degrees = compute_in_degrees(dag)
    sources = for {id, 0} <- in_degrees, do: id
    build_levels(dag, sources, in_degrees, [])
  end

  defp build_levels(_dag, [], _degrees, levels),
    do: Enum.reverse(levels)
  defp build_levels(dag, current_level, degrees, levels) do
    new_degrees =
      Enum.reduce(current_level, degrees, fn id, acc ->
        dag.edges
        |> Enum.filter(fn {from, _to} -> from == id end)
        |> Enum.reduce(acc, fn {_from, to}, inner_acc ->
          Map.update!(inner_acc, to, &(&1 - 1))
        end)
      end)

    next_level =
      for {id, 0} <- new_degrees,
          id not in List.flatten([current_level | levels]),
          do: id

    build_levels(dag, next_level, new_degrees,
                [current_level | levels])
  end
end

8.5 Reputation Engine

Listing 5: Six-dimensional reputation scoring
defmodule PlannerEngine.Reputation do
  use GenServer

  @dimensions [:accuracy, :completeness, :timeliness,
               :communication, :efficiency, :innovation]
  @default_weights List.duplicate(1/6, 6)
  @decay_factor 0.95

  def compute_score(agent_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:compute_score, agent_id})
  end

  def handle_call({:compute_score, agent_id}, _from, state) do
    history = Map.get(state.histories, agent_id, [])
    score = exponential_weighted_score(
              history, @default_weights, @decay_factor)
    {:reply, score, state}
  end

  defp exponential_weighted_score([], _w, _l), do: 0.5
  defp exponential_weighted_score(history, weights, lambda) do
    n = length(history)

    {weighted_sum, weight_total} =
      history
      |> Enum.with_index()
      |> Enum.reduce({0.0, 0.0}, fn {qv, i}, {ws, wt} ->
        decay = :math.pow(lambda, n - 1 - i)
        inner = Enum.zip(weights, qv)
                |> Enum.map(fn {w, q} -> w * q end)
                |> Enum.sum()
        {ws + decay * inner, wt + decay}
      end)

    weighted_sum / weight_total
  end
end

8.6 Market Module

Listing 6: Market clearing and revenue distribution
defmodule PlannerEngine.Market do
  use GenServer

  @operator_share 0.70
  @platform_share 0.15
  @llm_reserve_share 0.15

  def clear_market(task_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:clear_market, task_id})
  end

  def distribute_revenue(contract_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:distribute, contract_id})
  end

  defp do_distribute(contract) do
    total = compute_total(contract)
    %{
      operator: round(total * @operator_share),
      platform: round(total * @platform_share),
      llm_reserve: round(total * @llm_reserve_share)
    }
  end

  defp compute_total(%{pricing_model: :per_task} = c),
    do: c.rate_credits
  defp compute_total(%{pricing_model: :hourly} = c),
    do: c.rate_credits * ceil(c.hours_worked)
  defp compute_total(%{pricing_model: :per_token} = c),
    do: c.rate_per_token * c.tokens_consumed
end

9. Case Studies

9.1 AI Agent Marketplace: The AgentHero Model

The AgentHero platform implements the full framework described in this paper. We trace a concrete job through the categorical machinery.

Scenario

A client needs a web application tested. The job requires: functional testing (Playwright-based E2E tests), performance testing (k6 load tests), security scanning (OWASP ZAP), and accessibility audit (axe-core).

Step 1: Task functor

The client posts the job (status: draft). The task functor \(F\) maps it to requirements:

$$F(\tau) = (\{\text{playwright}, \text{k6}, \text{zap}, \text{axe}\}, \{t \leq 4\text{h}\}, 5000\text{ credits}, \text{24h deadline})$$

Step 2: Job decomposition

The orchestrator applies the decomposition functor \(\Delta\):

$$\Delta(\tau): \quad V = \{\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{a11y}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}\}$$ $$E = \{(\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{a11y}}, \sigma_{\text{report}})\}$$

The topological sort yields levels \(L_0 = \{\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{a11y}}\}\) (parallel) and \(L_1 = \{\sigma_{\text{report}}\}\) (barrier). The critical path length is 2.

Step 3: Order book

The job moves to open. Three operators submit proposals:

AgentCreditsDurationConfidenceReputation
\(\alpha_1\) (TestBot-Pro)35003h0.920.88
\(\alpha_2\) (QA-Agent)42002h0.850.91
\(\alpha_3\) (BugHunter)28005h0.780.72

Step 4: Matching

The cost functional from Corollary 4.2:

$$\text{cost}(\alpha_1) = \frac{3500}{1 + 0.92 \times 0.88} = \frac{3500}{1.8096} \approx 1934$$ $$\text{cost}(\alpha_2) = \frac{4200}{1 + 0.85 \times 0.91} = \frac{4200}{1.7735} \approx 2368$$ $$\text{cost}(\alpha_3) = \frac{2800}{1 + 0.78 \times 0.72} = \frac{2800}{1.5616} \approx 1793$$

\(\alpha_3\) has the lowest cost but the lowest confidence and reputation. The client selects \(\alpha_1\) (best cost-adjusted quality), triggering: (1) \(\alpha_2\) and \(\alpha_3\) auto-rejected (colimit universal property); (2) holdEscrow(client, 3500, contract_42) (escrow monad unit); (3) Contract created, job moves to in_progress.

Step 5: Execution

The Inngest pipeline executes the DAG. Level \(L_0\) runs four subtasks in parallel. Upon completion, barrier synchronization triggers \(L_1\) (report generation).

Step 6: Settlement

The client approves the deliverable. Revenue split:

$$\text{Operator } (\alpha_1): 0.70 \times 3500 = 2450 \text{ credits}$$ $$\text{Platform}: 0.15 \times 3500 = 525 \text{ credits}$$ $$\text{LLM reserve}: 0.15 \times 3500 = 525 \text{ credits}$$

Step 7: Reputation update

The evaluator scores quality: \(\mathbf{q} = (0.95, 0.90, 0.97, 0.88, 0.91, 0.82)\). Agent \(\alpha_1\)'s reputation is updated via the exponentially-weighted average.

9.2 Decentralized Task Allocation

Consider a scenario where no central marketplace exists. Agents discover tasks through a gossip protocol, and matching occurs via distributed consensus. The categorical framework still applies: the order book profunctor \(P\) is distributed across nodes with CRDT-based consistency; the escrow monad is implemented via a distributed ledger; and the natural transformation \(\eta\) is computed locally and verified globally. This demonstrates the framework's generality.

9.3 Multi-Agent Negotiation

In complex jobs, the decomposition functor \(\Delta\) produces a DAG where different subtasks are matched to different agents. The planner natural transformation \(\eta\) must then be a family of matchings:

$$\eta = \{\eta_{\sigma_i}: F(\sigma_i) \to G(M(\sigma_i))\}_{i=1}^{n}$$

The naturality condition ensures that agents assigned to dependent subtasks can communicate: if \((\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \in E\), then \(M(\sigma_i)\)'s output is compatible with \(M(\sigma_j)\)'s input.

10. Discussion

10.1 Comparison to Financial Exchanges

The key innovation is that AI task markets require quality verification before settlement, introducing a review phase absent in financial exchanges.

10.2 Comparison to Kubernetes Scheduler

The Kubernetes scheduler and the AI planner both solve assignment problems, but at different abstraction levels. Kubernetes assigns deterministic workloads to nodes; the AI planner assigns stochastic workloads to self-interested agents. The categorical framework subsumes both: Kubernetes scheduling is the special case where the escrow monad is trivial (\(E = \mathrm{id}\)) and the reputation functor is constant (\(\mathcal{R}(\alpha) = 1\) for all \(\alpha\)).

10.3 Mechanism Design Implications

The revelation principle suggests that our bidding mechanism can be replaced by a direct revelation mechanism. The VCG payment for agent \(\alpha_k\) winning task \(\tau\) is:

$$p_k^{\text{VCG}} = \texttt{cost}(\alpha_{k'}) - \sum_{j \neq k} \texttt{cost}(\alpha_j)$$

where \(\alpha_{k'}\) is the second-best agent. However, VCG has known vulnerabilities in repeated settings; our anti-gaming mechanisms address this by detecting collusion rings and suspicious patterns.

10.4 Limitations and Future Work

Bounded rationality. Our framework assumes agents maximize expected utility. LLM-based agents may exhibit bounded rationality. Behavioral mechanism design offers tools for handling such deviations.

Dynamic capabilities. Agent capabilities evolve over time. The capability functor \(G\) should be time-dependent: \(G_t: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{K}\), requiring indexed categories.

Multi-objective optimization. The cost functional is scalar. Pareto-optimal matching in a multi-criteria setting requires enriched profunctors over partially ordered monoids.

Privacy. Agents may not wish to reveal their true capabilities. Secure multi-party computation or zero-knowledge proofs could enable private matching while preserving the colimit structure.

11. Conclusion

We have presented a comprehensive categorical framework for the planner engine of an AI operating system, unifying task orchestration, marketplace dynamics, financial mechanics, and reputation management under a single mathematical roof.

The key constructions are:

  1. The order book profunctor \(P: \mathcal{A}^{\mathrm{op}} \times \mathcal{T} \to \mathbf{Set}\), capturing the two-sided market structure.
  2. The planner natural transformation \(\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M\), formalizing matching with provable consistency (naturality).
  3. The escrow monad \((E, \eta, \mu)\) on the credit category, with verified monad laws guaranteeing financial consistency.
  4. Market clearing as a colimit, characterizing acceptance and auto-rejection via the universal property.
  5. Functorial job decomposition into DAGs with topologically-sorted parallel execution.
  6. Convergent reputation scoring via exponentially-weighted 6-dimensional quality vectors with anti-gaming constraints.

The Elixir/OTP implementation demonstrates that these abstractions are not merely theoretical: GenServer order books, Mnesia-backed escrow transactions, and supervision-tree decomposition provide production-ready realizations. The planner engine occupies the highest level of the AI OS abstraction hierarchy. Part V completes the series by presenting the synthesis that unifies all four subsystems into a coherent 2-categorical framework.
