% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We present a categorical framework for the \emph{planner engine} of an AI operating system, establishing it as the highest-level orchestration primitive analogous to \texttt{systemd}/\texttt{init} in classical operating systems. The planner is formalized as a \emph{natural transformation} $\eta: F \Rightarrow G$ between the \emph{task functor} $F$, mapping jobs to requirement specifications, and the \emph{capability functor} $G$, mapping agents to offered capabilities. We introduce an \emph{order book} structure modeled as a profunctor $P: \Agents^{\op} \times \Tasks \to \Set$, through which agents (sellers) and clients (buyers) interact via a bidding protocol. Market clearing is characterized as a colimit in the transaction category, and the escrow mechanism is shown to satisfy the monad laws, guaranteeing financial consistency of credit operations. Job decomposition into directed acyclic graphs with topological execution ordering is proven to be functorial, preserving job semantics under decomposition. We validate the framework through an Elixir/OTP implementation featuring GenServer-based order books, Mnesia-backed escrow transactions, and supervision-tree-based task decomposition, drawing on the architecture of a production AI agent marketplace. The paper establishes that the marriage of market microstructure and category-theoretic planning yields provably correct orchestration with built-in economic incentives.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} AI planning, natural transformation, order book, profunctor, escrow monad, market microstructure, task decomposition, reputation systems, Elixir/OTP, agent marketplace
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Every operating system has a process that runs first and orchestrates everything else. In Unix, this is \texttt{init} (PID 1). In modern Linux, \texttt{systemd} manages service dependencies, socket activation, and lifecycle transitions. In Kubernetes, the scheduler assigns pods to nodes based on resource requests, affinity rules, and priority classes. These systems solve the same fundamental problem: given a set of work items and a set of executors, determine \emph{who does what, when, and how}.

An AI operating system faces this problem at a higher level of abstraction. The ``processes'' are not deterministic programs but stochastic agents with heterogeneous capabilities, varying reliability, and economic incentives. The ``scheduler'' must not only assign tasks but must negotiate prices, hold funds in escrow, verify quality, and update reputations. In short, the planner engine of an AI OS is simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item A \textbf{task decomposer} that breaks complex jobs into dependency graphs of subtasks;
    \item A \textbf{marketplace} where agents bid on work and clients select providers;
    \item A \textbf{financial system} managing credits, escrow, and revenue distribution;
    \item A \textbf{reputation engine} that scores quality and prevents gaming.
\end{enumerate}

This paper provides a unified categorical framework for all four aspects. The central insight is that planning is a \emph{natural transformation} between two functors: one that maps jobs to their requirements, and another that maps agents to their capabilities. The naturality condition ensures that planning decisions are consistent---changing the decomposition of a job and then matching is the same as matching and then decomposing the matched components.

The marketplace aspect is captured by an \emph{order book} modeled as a profunctor. Just as in financial markets, buy orders (task requests) and sell orders (agent proposals) accumulate until a matching occurs. The escrow mechanism, which holds client credits until work is verified, satisfies the monad laws, providing algebraic guarantees of financial consistency. Market clearing---the moment when a proposal is accepted and a contract is formed---is characterized as a colimit in the transaction category.

We ground every abstraction in a concrete system: the AgentHero marketplace, where jobs follow the lifecycle \texttt{draft} $\to$ \texttt{open} $\to$ \texttt{in\_progress} $\to$ \texttt{review}/\texttt{completed}/\texttt{cancelled}/\texttt{disputed}, operators submit proposals with execution plans and confidence scores, and atomic credit operations via Supabase RPC prevent race conditions. We provide a complete Elixir/OTP implementation demonstrating that the categorical structure maps directly to production-quality OTP patterns.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A formal market category $\Market$ with participants as objects and transactions as morphisms, with the order book as a profunctor (\Cref{sec:market-model}).
    \item The planner as a natural transformation $\eta: F \Rightarrow G$ between task and capability functors, with a proof that naturality implies planning consistency (\Cref{sec:planning-nat}).
    \item Functorial job decomposition into DAGs with topological execution ordering (\Cref{sec:decomposition}).

\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Process Orchestration in Classical Operating Systems}

The evolution of process orchestration follows a clear trajectory of increasing abstraction:

\paragraph{Unix init.} The original \texttt{init} process reads \texttt{/etc/inittab} and spawns services in a fixed order determined by runlevels. Dependencies are implicit in the ordering of startup scripts. This approach is inherently sequential and fragile---a slow service blocks all subsequent ones.

\paragraph{systemd.} Poettering and Sievers' \texttt{systemd} \cite{poettering2010systemd} introduced a declarative dependency graph. Each unit file specifies \texttt{Requires}, \texttt{After}, \texttt{Wants}, and \texttt{Conflicts} relationships. The dependency graph is topologically sorted, and independent services start in parallel. Socket activation allows services to start on-demand. This is a significant advance: the scheduler reasons about a DAG of dependencies rather than a linear sequence.

\paragraph{Kubernetes.} The Kubernetes scheduler \cite{burns2016borg} operates at the cluster level. It maintains a queue of unscheduled pods and iterates through a pipeline of \emph{predicates} (hard constraints: resource availability, node affinity, taints/tolerations) and \emph{priorities} (soft preferences: load balancing, data locality). The scheduler is extensible via scheduling frameworks and plugins. Critically, Kubernetes separates the \emph{desired state} (declared in manifests) from the \emph{actual state} (observed by controllers), with reconciliation loops driving convergence.

\paragraph{From orchestration to markets.} Each of these systems assumes a benevolent, centralized scheduler that allocates resources optimally. In an AI agent ecosystem, this assumption breaks down. Agents are autonomous, self-interested entities. They may misrepresent capabilities, deliver substandard work, or collude. The planner must therefore incorporate market mechanisms---pricing, bidding, escrow, reputation---that align individual incentives with system-wide efficiency.

\subsection{Market Microstructure}

Market microstructure theory \cite{ohara1995market,harris2003trading} studies how trading mechanisms affect price formation, liquidity, and information aggregation. Key concepts include:

\paragraph{Order books.} A limit order book (LOB) maintains buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks), each specifying a price and quantity. Orders are matched when a bid price meets or exceeds an ask price. The \emph{spread} between the best bid and best ask reflects liquidity and information asymmetry.

\paragraph{Matching engines.} Matching follows priority rules: price-time priority (best price first, then earliest arrival) is standard. Pro-rata matching allocates proportionally at the same price level. In our setting, ``price'' is the credit cost and ``quantity'' is a unit of agent work.

\paragraph{Market clearing.} A market clears when supply equals demand at some price. In continuous markets, clearing occurs order-by-order. In call auctions, orders accumulate and clear simultaneously at a single price. Our order book supports both modes.

\paragraph{Mechanism design.} The revelation principle \cite{myerson1981optimal} states that any mechanism can be replicated by a direct revelation mechanism where truthful reporting is incentive-compatible. The Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) mechanism \cite{vickrey1961counterspeculation,clarke1971multipart,groves1973incentives} achieves efficiency and truthfulness for combinatorial auctions. We draw on these results to design anti-gaming mechanisms for agent reputation.

\subsection{Category Theory Preliminaries}

We assume familiarity with categories, functors, and natural transformations at the level of Mac Lane \cite{maclane1998categories}. We briefly recall the structures central to this paper.

\begin{definition}[Profunctor]
A \emph{profunctor} $P: \cat{C}^{\op} \times \cat{D} \to \Set$ is a functor from the product of the opposite of $\cat{C}$ with $\cat{D}$ to the category of sets. It generalizes the notion of a relation between categories. For objects $c \in \cat{C}$ and $d \in \cat{D}$, $P(c, d)$ is the set of ``heteromorphisms'' from $c$ to $d$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Monad]
A \emph{monad} on a category $\cat{C}$ is a triple $(T, \mu, \eta)$ where $T: \cat{C} \to \cat{C}$ is an endofunctor, $\eta: \id_{\cat{C}} \Rightarrow T$ is the unit, and $\mu: T^2 \Rightarrow T$ is the multiplication, satisfying:
\begin{align}
    \mu \circ T\mu &= \mu \circ \mu T \quad \text{(associativity)} \label{eq:monad-assoc} \\
    \mu \circ T\eta &= \mu \circ \eta T = \id_T \quad \text{(unit laws)} \label{eq:monad-unit}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Colimit]
Given a diagram $D: \cat{J} \to \cat{C}$, a \emph{colimit} of $D$ is an object $\colim D$ in $\cat{C}$ together with a cocone $\{\iota_j: D(j) \to \colim D\}_{j \in \cat{J}}$ that is universal: for any other cocone $\{\phi_j: D(j) \to X\}_{j \in \cat{J}}$, there exists a unique morphism $u: \colim D \to X$ with $u \circ \iota_j = \phi_j$ for all $j$.
\end{definition}

\section{Market Model --- Formal Framework}\label{sec:market-model}

We now construct the categorical framework for the agent marketplace. The fundamental insight is that a marketplace is a category whose objects are economic participants and whose morphisms are transactions between them.

\subsection{The Market Category}

\begin{definition}[Market Category]\label{def:market-cat}
The \emph{market category} $\Market$ is defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objects:} Economic participants, partitioned into \emph{clients} $\mathcal{B}$ (buyers), \emph{operators} $\mathcal{S}$ (sellers/agents), and the \emph{platform} $\pi$. Thus $\Ob(\Market) = \mathcal{B} \sqcup \mathcal{S} \sqcup \{\pi\}$.
    \item \textbf{Morphisms:} A morphism $f: a \to b$ is a \emph{transaction} transferring credits from participant $a$ to participant $b$. Each morphism carries:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item An amount $\mathrm{amt}(f) \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$;
        \item A timestamp $\mathrm{time}(f) \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$;
        \item A transaction type $\mathrm{type}(f) \in \{\texttt{escrow\_hold}, \texttt{escrow\_release}, \texttt{payment}, \texttt{refund}, \texttt{fee}\}$.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Composition:} Sequential transactions compose: if $f: a \to b$ and $g: b \to c$, then $g \circ f: a \to c$ with $\mathrm{amt}(g \circ f) = \mathrm{amt}(g)$ (the final transfer amount).
    \item \textbf{Identity:} The identity $\id_a: a \to a$ is the zero-transfer transaction.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The market category is enriched over $(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, +, 0)$: the hom-sets carry a monoidal structure given by addition of transaction amounts. This enrichment captures the financial flow structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Order Book as a Profunctor}

In a classical limit order book, buy orders and sell orders are two-sided. We generalize this to a profunctor relating agents and tasks.

\begin{definition}[Agent and Task Categories]\label{def:agent-task-cat}
Define the \emph{agent category} $\Agents$ and the \emph{task category} $\Tasks$ as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Agents$: Objects are agents (operators). A morphism $\alpha \to \alpha'$ exists when agent $\alpha'$ can simulate or subsume the capabilities of $\alpha$ (capability ordering).
    \item $\Tasks$: Objects are tasks (jobs or subtasks). A morphism $\tau \to \tau'$ exists when task $\tau$ is a prerequisite of $\tau'$ (dependency ordering).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Order Book Profunctor]\label{def:order-book}
The \emph{order book} is a profunctor
\[
    P: \Agents^{\op} \times \Tasks \to \Set
\]
where $P(\alpha, \tau)$ is the set of \emph{proposals} (sell orders) from agent $\alpha$ for task $\tau$. Each proposal $p \in P(\alpha, \tau)$ consists of:
\begin{align}
    p = (\texttt{execution\_plan}, \texttt{estimated\_credits}, \texttt{estimated\_duration}, \texttt{confidence\_score})
\end{align}
The profunctor action is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Contravariant in $\Agents$:} If $\alpha' \to \alpha$ (i.e., $\alpha$ subsumes $\alpha'$), then any proposal from $\alpha'$ can be lifted to a proposal from $\alpha$: $P(\alpha', \tau) \to P(\alpha, \tau)$.
    \item \textbf{Covariant in $\Tasks$:} If $\tau \to \tau'$ (i.e., $\tau$ is a prerequisite of $\tau'$), then completing $\tau$ enables proposals for $\tau'$: there is an induced map $P(\alpha, \tau) \to P(\alpha, \tau')$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Dually, we define the \emph{demand side}:

\begin{definition}[Demand Profunctor]\label{def:demand}
The \emph{demand profunctor} is
\[
    Q: \mathcal{B}^{\op} \times \Tasks \to \Set
\]
where $Q(b, \tau)$ is the set of job postings by client $b$ for task $\tau$, each specifying a budget ceiling, required capabilities, and deadline.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Market Clearing as a Colimit}

When a client accepts a proposal, we form a \emph{contract}. This acceptance event is characterized as a colimit.

\begin{definition}[Transaction Diagram]\label{def:tx-diagram}
Given a task $\tau$ with demand $q \in Q(b, \tau)$ and proposals $\{p_i \in P(\alpha_i, \tau)\}_{i \in I}$, the \emph{transaction diagram} is the functor $D: \cat{J} \to \Market$ where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\cat{J}$ is the index category with objects $\{b, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n, \pi\}$ and morphisms representing potential transactions;
    \item $D$ maps each object to the corresponding participant in $\Market$ and each morphism to a proposed transaction.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Market Clearing as Colimit]\label{thm:clearing-colimit}
Let $D: \cat{J} \to \Market$ be a transaction diagram for task $\tau$. The market clearing event---where one proposal $p_k$ is accepted and all others $\{p_i\}_{i \neq k}$ are rejected---is the colimit $\colim D$ in $\Market$. The universal cocone consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The escrow morphism $e: b \to \pi$ with $\mathrm{amt}(e) = \texttt{estimated\_credits}(p_k)$;
    \item The contract formation $c: \pi \to \alpha_k$ (pending work completion);
    \item Rejection morphisms $r_i: \alpha_i \to \alpha_i$ (identity, no transaction) for $i \neq k$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We verify the universal property. Let $\{e, c, r_i\}$ be the proposed cocone. For any other cocone $\{\phi_j\}_{j \in \cat{J}}$ over the same diagram, the unique mediating morphism $u: \colim D \to X$ is determined by the contract terms: the escrow amount uniquely determines the payment flow, and the accepted proposal uniquely determines the executor. The rejection morphisms are identities, so they factor trivially. The uniqueness of $u$ follows from the budget ceiling constraint---only one allocation of the client's credits is consistent with accepting exactly one proposal.

To show existence, observe that accepting proposal $p_k$ induces:
\begin{align}
    \texttt{holdEscrow}(b, \texttt{estimated\_credits}(p_k), \texttt{contract\_id}) \label{eq:escrow-hold}
\end{align}
which atomically decrements the client's balance and creates an escrow record. This atomic operation, implemented via Supabase RPC, ensures that the colimit exists and is unique (no double-spending).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:auto-reject}
In the colimit construction, accepting proposal $p_k$ necessarily rejects all other proposals $\{p_i\}_{i \neq k}$. This is the universal property at work: the cocone maps are uniquely determined.
\end{corollary}

This matches the AgentHero implementation exactly: when a client accepts one proposal, all others are auto-rejected, and a contract is created with escrowed credits.

\subsection{The Escrow Monad}

\begin{definition}[Credit Category]\label{def:credit-cat}
The \emph{credit category} $\Credits$ has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Objects: credit balances $(b, n)$ where $b$ is a participant and $n \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is the balance;
    \item Morphisms: balance transitions $(b, n) \to (b, m)$ representing credit/debit operations.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Escrow Monad]\label{def:escrow-monad}
The \emph{escrow monad} $E: \Credits \to \Credits$ is the endofunctor defined by:
\begin{align}
    E(b, n) &= (b, n - h, h) \quad \text{where } h \text{ is the held amount} \\
    \eta_{(b,n)} &: (b, n) \to E(b, n) = (b, n, 0) \quad \text{(unit: no hold)} \\
    \mu_{(b,n,h)} &: E(E(b, n)) \to E(b, n) \quad \text{(join: flatten nested holds)}
\end{align}
The monadic operations correspond to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unit ($\eta$):} Initialize escrow with zero hold. ``return'' in Haskell.
    \item \textbf{Bind ($\gg\!\!=$):} Sequence escrow operations. Holding credits for contract $c_1$ and then for $c_2$ composes into a single escrow state.
    \item \textbf{Join ($\mu$):} Flatten $E(E(b, n))$ to $E(b, n)$---settle an escrow-within-escrow (e.g., subcontracting).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\section{Planning as Natural Transformation}\label{sec:planning-nat}

We now formalize the planner as the central natural transformation connecting what needs to be done with who can do it.

\subsection{The Task Functor}

\begin{definition}[Requirement Category]\label{def:req-cat}
The \emph{requirement category} $\Req$ has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Objects: requirement specifications $r = (\texttt{capabilities}, \texttt{constraints}, \texttt{budget}, \texttt{deadline})$;
    \item Morphisms: requirement refinements $r \to r'$ where $r'$ is a stricter specification (more capabilities required, tighter constraints, etc.).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Task Functor]\label{def:task-functor}
The \emph{task functor} $F: \Tasks \to \Req$ maps each task to its requirement specification:
\begin{align}
    F(\tau) &= (\texttt{required\_capabilities}(\tau), \texttt{constraints}(\tau), \texttt{budget}(\tau), \texttt{deadline}(\tau))
\end{align}
For a morphism $\tau \to \tau'$ (dependency), $F$ maps it to the induced requirement refinement: if $\tau$ is a prerequisite of $\tau'$, then the requirements of $\tau'$ refine those of $\tau$ (building on completed prerequisites).
\end{definition}

\subsection{The Capability Functor}

\begin{definition}[Capability Category]\label{def:cap-cat}
The \emph{capability category} $\Cap$ has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Objects: capability profiles $k = (\texttt{skills}, \texttt{capacity}, \texttt{pricing}, \texttt{reputation})$;
    \item Morphisms: capability enhancements $k \to k'$ where $k'$ has additional skills or capacity.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Capability Functor]\label{def:cap-functor}
The \emph{capability functor} $G: \Agents \to \Cap$ maps each agent to its capability profile:
\begin{align}
    G(\alpha) &= (\texttt{skills}(\alpha), \texttt{capacity}(\alpha), \texttt{pricing\_model}(\alpha), \texttt{reputation}(\alpha))
\end{align}
where $\texttt{pricing\_model} \in \{\texttt{per\_task}, \texttt{hourly}, \texttt{per\_token}\}$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The Planner Natural Transformation}

\begin{definition}[Planner]\label{def:planner}
The \emph{planner} is a natural transformation
\[
    \eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M
\]
where $M: \Tasks \to \Agents$ is the matching functor (assigning agents to tasks). For each task $\tau$, the component $\eta_\tau: F(\tau) \to G(M(\tau))$ witnesses that the assigned agent's capabilities satisfy the task's requirements.
\end{definition}

In the expanded form, this is a natural transformation in the following commutative diagram. For every morphism $f: \tau \to \tau'$ in $\Tasks$ (a dependency relation):

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=4em, row sep=3em]
    F(\tau) \arrow[r, "\eta_\tau"] \arrow[d, "F(f)"'] & G(M(\tau)) \arrow[d, "G(M(f))"] \\
    F(\tau') \arrow[r, "\eta_{\tau'}"'] & G(M(\tau'))
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}

\begin{theorem}[Planning Consistency]\label{thm:planning-consistency}
Let $\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M$ be the planner natural transformation. The naturality condition
\[
    G(M(f)) \circ \eta_\tau = \eta_{\tau'} \circ F(f)
\]
for every dependency $f: \tau \to \tau'$ guarantees \emph{planning consistency}: the following two procedures yield the same result:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Refine the requirements of $\tau$ to those of $\tau'$ (via $F(f)$), then match $\tau'$ to an agent (via $\eta_{\tau'}$).
    \item Match $\tau$ to an agent (via $\eta_\tau$), then enhance the agent's capability commitment from that of $\tau$ to that of $\tau'$ (via $G(M(f))$).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is precisely the naturality square. The condition states that the diagram commutes, i.e., the two paths from $F(\tau)$ to $G(M(\tau'))$ are equal. Concretely, this means that planning decisions are stable under requirement refinement. If task $\tau$ is a prerequisite of $\tau'$, then the agent assigned to handle the chain $\tau \to \tau'$ must be capable of handling the refined requirements. This prevents the situation where an agent is assigned to $\tau$ but cannot handle the downstream task $\tau'$ that depends on $\tau$'s output.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Optimal Matching]\label{cor:optimal-matching}
The optimal matching functor $M^*$ minimizes the cost functional
\[
    M^* = \argmin_{M} \sum_{\tau \in \Ob(\Tasks)} \texttt{cost}(\eta_\tau)
\]
subject to the naturality constraint $G(M(f)) \circ \eta_\tau = \eta_{\tau'} \circ F(f)$ for all dependencies $f$, where $\texttt{cost}(\eta_\tau)$ incorporates the estimated credits, inverse confidence score, and reputation discount:
\[
    \texttt{cost}(\eta_\tau) = \frac{\texttt{estimated\_credits}(M(\tau), \tau)}{1 + \texttt{confidence\_score}(M(\tau), \tau) \cdot \texttt{reputation}(M(\tau))}
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The cost functional is well-defined on the space of natural transformations $\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M$ because the naturality constraint restricts $M$ to those matchings that respect task dependencies. The minimum exists because the set of agents is finite, the tasks are finite, and the cost functional is bounded below by zero.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Matching Process}

In the AgentHero system, matching proceeds through the following stages, each of which we interpret categorically:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Job posting (draft $\to$ open):} A client creates a job with requirements $F(\tau)$. This defines the left-hand side of the naturality square.

    \item \textbf{Proposal submission:} Operators inspect $F(\tau)$ and submit proposals $p \in P(\alpha, \tau)$. Each proposal is a candidate component $\eta_\tau^{(\alpha)}: F(\tau) \to G(\alpha)$.

    \item \textbf{Proposal evaluation:} The client evaluates proposals. This is a selection over the fiber $P(-, \tau) = \coprod_\alpha P(\alpha, \tau)$.

    \item \textbf{Acceptance (open $\to$ in\_progress):} The client accepts proposal $p_k$ from agent $\alpha_k$. This selects the component $\eta_\tau = \eta_\tau^{(\alpha_k)}$ and defines $M(\tau) = \alpha_k$. All other proposals are auto-rejected (\Cref{cor:auto-reject}).

    \item \textbf{Contract creation:} The acceptance triggers $\texttt{holdEscrow}(b, \texttt{rate\_credits}, \texttt{contract\_id})$, applying the escrow monad unit.

    \item \textbf{Execution and review (in\_progress $\to$ review):} The agent executes the task. Upon completion, the work enters review.

    \item \textbf{Settlement (review $\to$ completed):} Approval releases escrow via the monad join, distributing credits: 70\% operator, 15\% platform, 15\% LLM reserve.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Job Decomposition}\label{sec:decomposition}

Complex jobs must be broken into manageable subtasks. The AgentHero orchestrator performs this decomposition, producing a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of subtasks with dependency edges.

\subsection{The Decomposition Functor}

\begin{definition}[DAG Category]\label{def:dag-cat}
Let $\cat{DAG}$ be the category whose objects are directed acyclic graphs $(V, E)$ with $V$ a finite set of vertices (subtasks) and $E \subseteq V \times V$ the edge set (dependencies). A morphism $\phi: (V, E) \to (V', E')$ is a graph homomorphism preserving the partial order.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Decomposition Functor]\label{def:decomp-functor}
The \emph{decomposition functor} $\Delta: \Tasks \to \cat{DAG}$ maps each task $\tau$ to its decomposition graph $\Delta(\tau) = (V_\tau, E_\tau)$, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $V_\tau = \{\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n\}$ are the subtasks;
    \item $(\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \in E_\tau$ iff $\sigma_i$ must complete before $\sigma_j$ begins;
    \item For a dependency $f: \tau \to \tau'$, the morphism $\Delta(f): \Delta(\tau) \to \Delta(\tau')$ maps terminal subtasks of $\Delta(\tau)$ to initial subtasks of $\Delta(\tau')$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Decomposition Preserves Job Semantics]\label{prop:decomp-functorial}
The decomposition functor $\Delta$ is faithful: distinct tasks have distinct decompositions, and the dependency structure is preserved. Formally:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\Delta$ preserves identities: $\Delta(\id_\tau) = \id_{\Delta(\tau)}$.
    \item $\Delta$ preserves composition: $\Delta(g \circ f) = \Delta(g) \circ \Delta(f)$ for composable dependencies $f, g$.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, executing the decomposed subtasks in any topological order that respects $E_\tau$ is equivalent to executing the original task $\tau$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Preservation of identities: the identity dependency $\id_\tau: \tau \to \tau$ maps to the identity graph homomorphism on $\Delta(\tau)$.

Preservation of composition: let $f: \tau_1 \to \tau_2$ and $g: \tau_2 \to \tau_3$. Then $\Delta(f)$ connects terminal subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_1)$ to initial subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_2)$, and $\Delta(g)$ connects terminal subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_2)$ to initial subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_3)$. The composition $\Delta(g) \circ \Delta(f)$ is the graph homomorphism that connects terminal subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_1)$ through $\Delta(\tau_2)$ to initial subtasks of $\Delta(\tau_3)$, which is exactly $\Delta(g \circ f)$.

Faithfulness: distinct dependencies induce distinct graph homomorphisms because the subtask structure carries strictly more information than the coarse dependency.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Topological Sort for Execution Ordering}

Given a DAG $\Delta(\tau) = (V_\tau, E_\tau)$, we compute a topological ordering $\sigma_{i_1}, \sigma_{i_2}, \ldots, \sigma_{i_n}$ such that $(\sigma_{i_j}, \sigma_{i_k}) \in E_\tau \implies j < k$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\caption{Topological Sort with Parallel Barrier Detection}\label{alg:topsort}
\KwIn{DAG $(V, E)$ with $|V| = n$}
\KwOut{Execution schedule $S = [L_1, L_2, \ldots, L_m]$ where each $L_i$ is a parallel execution level}
Compute in-degrees: $\forall v \in V, \deg^-(v) = |\{u : (u, v) \in E\}|$\;
$L_0 \leftarrow \{v \in V : \deg^-(v) = 0\}$ \tcp{sources: no dependencies}
$S \leftarrow [L_0]$\;
$i \leftarrow 0$\;
\While{$\bigcup_{j \leq i} L_j \neq V$}{
    $L_{i+1} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \ForEach{$v \in L_i$}{
        \ForEach{$(v, w) \in E$}{
            $\deg^-(w) \leftarrow \deg^-(w) - 1$\;
            \If{$\deg^-(w) = 0$}{
                $L_{i+1} \leftarrow L_{i+1} \cup \{w\}$\;
            }
        }
    }
    $S \leftarrow S \cup [L_{i+1}]$\;
    $i \leftarrow i + 1$\;
}
\Return{$S$}
\end{algorithm}

Each level $L_i$ consists of subtasks that can execute in parallel---their dependencies are all satisfied by the completion of levels $L_0, \ldots, L_{i-1}$. The transition from $L_i$ to $L_{i+1}$ is a \emph{barrier synchronization}: all tasks in $L_i$ must complete before any task in $L_{i+1}$ begins.

\begin{proposition}[Optimality of Level-Based Parallelism]\label{prop:parallel-optimal}
The number of levels $m$ in \Cref{alg:topsort} equals the length of the longest path in the DAG. This is the theoretical minimum number of sequential steps required, and is therefore optimal for parallel execution.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each level contains all vertices whose longest incoming path has the same length. The critical path (longest path from any source to any sink) determines $m$. No schedule can execute in fewer than $m$ steps, because the critical path tasks are sequentially dependent.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Integration with Inngest Durable Execution}

In the AgentHero implementation, each subtask execution is wrapped in an Inngest function, providing:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Event-driven triggers:} Subtask $\sigma_j$ fires an event upon completion; dependent subtasks $\sigma_k$ are triggered by this event.
    \item \textbf{Automatic retries:} Transient failures (LLM timeouts, rate limits) are retried with exponential backoff.
    \item \textbf{Idempotency:} Each subtask execution is idempotent, preventing duplicate work on retry.
    \item \textbf{Observability:} The execution trace is a reified version of the DAG with timing information.
\end{itemize}

The categorical interpretation is a functor from the DAG category to the category of Inngest functions:
\[
    \Phi: \cat{DAG} \to \cat{Inngest}
\]
where $\cat{Inngest}$ is the category of event-driven durable functions with composition given by event chaining.

\section{Financial Mechanics}\label{sec:financial}

\subsection{The Escrow Monad in Detail}

We now prove that the escrow mechanism satisfies the monad laws, providing algebraic guarantees of financial consistency.

\begin{definition}[Escrow Endofunctor]\label{def:escrow-endo}
Define $E: \Credits \to \Credits$ on objects by
\[
    E(b, n) = (b, n_{\text{available}}, n_{\text{held}})
\]
where $n = n_{\text{available}} + n_{\text{held}}$, and on morphisms by
\[
    E(f: (b,n) \to (b,m)) = (f_{\text{avail}}: n_{\text{available}} \to m_{\text{available}},\; f_{\text{held}}: n_{\text{held}} \to m_{\text{held}})
\]
preserving the invariant $n_{\text{available}} + n_{\text{held}} = m_{\text{available}} + m_{\text{held}}$ (credits are conserved).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Escrow Unit and Multiplication]\label{def:escrow-unit-mult}
The \emph{unit} $\eta: \id_{\Credits} \Rightarrow E$ is:
\[
    \eta_{(b,n)}: (b, n) \mapsto (b, n, 0) \quad \text{(no credits held)}
\]

The \emph{multiplication} $\mu: E^2 \Rightarrow E$ flattens nested escrow:
\[
    \mu_{(b,n)}: E(E(b, n)) = (b, n_{\text{avail}}, (n_{\text{held\_outer}}, n_{\text{held\_inner}})) \mapsto (b, n_{\text{avail}}, n_{\text{held\_outer}} + n_{\text{held\_inner}})
\]
This corresponds to settling a subcontract escrow into the parent escrow.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Escrow Monad Laws]\label{thm:escrow-monad}
The triple $(E, \eta, \mu)$ satisfies the monad laws:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Left unit:} $\mu \circ \eta E = \id_E$
    \item \textbf{Right unit:} $\mu \circ E\eta = \id_E$
    \item \textbf{Associativity:} $\mu \circ \mu E = \mu \circ E\mu$
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Left unit.} Starting from $E(b,n) = (b, n_a, n_h)$:
\begin{align}
    (\mu \circ \eta E)(b, n_a, n_h) &= \mu(\eta(b, n_a, n_h)) \\
    &= \mu(b, (n_a, n_h), 0) \\
    &= (b, n_a, n_h + 0) = (b, n_a, n_h) = \id_E(b, n_a, n_h)
\end{align}

\textbf{Right unit.} Starting from $E(b,n) = (b, n_a, n_h)$:
\begin{align}
    (\mu \circ E\eta)(b, n_a, n_h) &= \mu(E(\eta)(b, n_a, n_h)) \\
    &= \mu(b, n_a, (n_h, 0)) \\
    &= (b, n_a, n_h + 0) = (b, n_a, n_h) = \id_E(b, n_a, n_h)
\end{align}

\textbf{Associativity.} Starting from $E^3(b,n)$ with three levels of holding $(h_1, h_2, h_3)$:
\begin{align}
    (\mu \circ \mu E)(b, n_a, (h_1, (h_2, h_3))) &= \mu(b, n_a, (h_1, h_2 + h_3)) = (b, n_a, h_1 + h_2 + h_3) \\
    (\mu \circ E\mu)(b, n_a, (h_1, (h_2, h_3))) &= \mu(b, n_a, (h_1 + h_2, h_3)) = (b, n_a, h_1 + h_2 + h_3)
\end{align}
Both paths yield the same result, since addition is associative.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Revenue Split as Natural Transformation}

Upon contract completion, escrowed credits are distributed:

\begin{definition}[Revenue Split]\label{def:revenue-split}
The \emph{revenue split} is a natural transformation $\rho: E \Rightarrow D$ where $D: \Credits \to \Credits^3$ is the distribution functor mapping a single credit pool to a triple:
\[
    D(n_{\text{held}}) = (0.70 \cdot n_{\text{held}},\; 0.15 \cdot n_{\text{held}},\; 0.15 \cdot n_{\text{held}})
\]
corresponding to (operator payment, platform fee, LLM reserve).
\end{definition}

The three pricing models affect how $n_{\text{held}}$ is computed:
\begin{align}
    n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{per\_task}} &= \texttt{fixed\_price} \\
    n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{hourly}} &= \texttt{rate\_credits} \times \lceil \texttt{hours\_worked} \rceil \\
    n_{\text{held}}^{\texttt{per\_token}} &= \texttt{rate\_per\_token} \times \texttt{tokens\_consumed}
\end{align}
In all cases, $n_{\text{held}} \leq \texttt{budget\_ceiling}$ to prevent overspend.

\subsection{Atomic Operations and Race Condition Prevention}

The escrow operations must be atomic to prevent race conditions. Consider two concurrent contracts attempting to hold credits from the same client balance:

\begin{definition}[Atomic Credit Operations]\label{def:atomic-ops}
An \emph{atomic credit operation} is a morphism in $\Credits$ that is implemented via a database-level serializable transaction. In the AgentHero system, this is a Supabase RPC call:
\begin{verbatim}
    adminClient.rpc('hold_escrow', {
      p_client_id: client_id,
      p_amount: rate_credits,
      p_contract_id: contract_id
    })
\end{verbatim}
The RPC function executes within a PostgreSQL transaction with \texttt{SERIALIZABLE} isolation, ensuring:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The balance check and deduction are atomic (no TOCTOU race);
    \item Negative balances are prevented by a \texttt{CHECK(balance >= 0)} constraint;
    \item Concurrent operations are serialized by the database.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Financial Consistency]\label{thm:financial-consistency}
Under the escrow monad with atomic credit operations, the following invariants hold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Conservation:} The total credits in the system (available + held + distributed) is constant.
    \item \textbf{Non-negativity:} No participant's available balance is ever negative.
    \item \textbf{Escrow completeness:} Every held amount is eventually either released (completed) or refunded (cancelled/disputed).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Conservation} follows from the monad laws: $\eta$ preserves totals (holds move credits from available to held, not creating or destroying them), and $\mu$ flattens without changing totals.

\textbf{Non-negativity} is enforced by the database constraint \texttt{CHECK(balance >= 0)}. The atomic RPC rolls back if this constraint would be violated.

\textbf{Escrow completeness} follows from the job lifecycle being a finite state machine with terminal states $\{\texttt{completed}, \texttt{cancelled}, \texttt{disputed}\}$. Each terminal state triggers either release or refund. With Inngest durable execution, liveness is guaranteed: the event-driven pipeline will eventually reach a terminal state (possibly via timeout).
\end{proof}

\section{Reputation and Trust}\label{sec:reputation}

\subsection{Six-Dimensional Quality Scoring}

After each job completion, the evaluator computes a quality vector capturing multiple dimensions of performance.

\begin{definition}[Quality Vector]\label{def:quality-vector}
The \emph{quality vector} for a completed job is $\mathbf{q} \in [0, 1]^6$ with components:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{q} = (q_{\text{accuracy}},\; q_{\text{completeness}},\; q_{\text{timeliness}},\; q_{\text{communication}},\; q_{\text{efficiency}},\; q_{\text{innovation}})
\end{align}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $q_{\text{accuracy}}$: correctness of deliverables against requirements;
    \item $q_{\text{completeness}}$: fraction of requirements addressed;
    \item $q_{\text{timeliness}}$: $\max(0, 1 - \frac{\text{actual\_duration} - \text{estimated\_duration}}{\text{deadline}})$;
    \item $q_{\text{communication}}$: responsiveness and clarity (client-rated);
    \item $q_{\text{efficiency}}$: $1 - \frac{\text{actual\_credits} - \text{estimated\_credits}}{\text{budget\_ceiling}}$;
    \item $q_{\text{innovation}}$: exceeding requirements or novel approaches (evaluator-scored).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Reputation Score]\label{def:reputation}
The \emph{reputation score} of agent $\alpha$ after $N$ completed jobs is the exponentially-weighted inner product:
\begin{align}
    R(\alpha) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \lambda^{N-i} \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{q}_i \rangle}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \lambda^{N-i}}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^6_{\geq 0}$ with $\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 = 1$ is a weight vector (default: uniform $\mathbf{w} = (\frac{1}{6}, \ldots, \frac{1}{6})$), and $\lambda \in (0, 1)$ is the decay factor (default: $\lambda = 0.95$), giving more weight to recent performance.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Reputation as a Functor}

\begin{definition}[Reputation Functor]\label{def:rep-functor}
The \emph{reputation functor} $\mathcal{R}: \Agents \to [0, 1]$ maps each agent to its reputation score and each capability enhancement morphism $\alpha \to \alpha'$ to the induced reputation update. The codomain $[0,1]$ is viewed as a poset category (with the usual ordering $\leq$).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Reputation Convergence]\label{prop:rep-convergence}
Under honest participation (i.e., quality vectors $\mathbf{q}_i$ are drawn i.i.d. from a distribution with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and finite variance), the reputation score converges:
\[
    R(\alpha) \xrightarrow{N \to \infty} \langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle
\]
almost surely.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The exponentially-weighted average is a consistent estimator of $\langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle$. Writing $X_i = \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{q}_i \rangle$, we have $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \langle \mathbf{w}, \boldsymbol{\mu} \rangle$ and
\[
    R(\alpha) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i}} = \frac{1 - \lambda}{1 - \lambda^N} \sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i
\]
As $N \to \infty$, $\frac{1-\lambda}{1-\lambda^N} \to (1-\lambda)$ and $\sum_{i=1}^N \lambda^{N-i} X_i$ is a geometrically-weighted sum. By the strong law of large numbers for weighted averages (see \cite{jamison1965convergence}), convergence holds a.s. provided $\text{Var}(X_i) < \infty$, which is guaranteed since $X_i \in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Anti-Gaming Mechanisms}

Gaming the reputation system---inflating scores through fake jobs, collusion, or strategic behavior---is modeled as a constraint satisfaction problem.

\begin{definition}[Gaming Detection]\label{def:gaming}
An agent $\alpha$ is flagged for \emph{suspected gaming} if any of the following anomaly detectors fire:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Self-dealing:} $\alpha$ and the client share a wallet address or IP cluster.
    \item \textbf{Score inflation:} The quality variance $\text{Var}(\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_N) < \epsilon$ for suspiciously many jobs (all scores are nearly identical).
    \item \textbf{Rapid cycling:} Jobs complete in less than $\delta_{\min}$ time with perfect scores.
    \item \textbf{Collusion ring:} A clique of agents and clients who only transact with each other, detected via graph analysis of the transaction subgraph.
    \item \textbf{Reputation laundering:} Creating a new agent identity after accumulating negative reputation.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The trust score incorporates both reputation and anti-gaming signals:

\begin{definition}[Trust Score]\label{def:trust}
The \emph{trust score} is:
\[
    T(\alpha) = R(\alpha) \cdot (1 - \gamma(\alpha)) \cdot \min\left(1, \frac{N(\alpha)}{N_{\min}}\right)
\]
where $\gamma(\alpha) \in [0, 1]$ is the gaming suspicion level, and the third factor is a ``seasoning'' term requiring a minimum number of jobs $N_{\min}$ before full trust is granted.
\end{definition}

\section{Elixir/OTP Implementation}\label{sec:implementation}

We now present a production-quality Elixir implementation of the planner engine, demonstrating that the categorical abstractions map directly to OTP patterns. The implementation consists of five modules: the order book (GenServer with matching engine), the escrow system (GenServer with Mnesia transactions), the task decomposer (with supervision trees), the reputation engine, and the market module.

\subsection{Application Architecture}

The application follows standard OTP conventions with a supervision tree:

defmodule PlannerEngine.Application do

  def start(_type, _args) do

\subsection{Order Book GenServer}

The order book maintains buy and sell sides, with price-time priority matching:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Order book GenServer (simplified)},label={lst:orderbook}]
defmodule PlannerEngine.OrderBook do
  use GenServer

  @type proposal :: %{
    agent_id: String.t(),
    task_id: String.t(),
    execution_plan: String.t(),
    estimated_credits: non_neg_integer(),
    estimated_duration: non_neg_integer(),
    confidence_score: float(),
    timestamp: DateTime.t()
  }

  defstruct proposals: %{}, demands: %{}, matches: []

  # Submit proposal (sell order)
  def submit_proposal(proposal) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:submit_proposal, proposal})
  end

  # Post demand (buy order)
  def post_demand(demand) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:post_demand, demand})
  end

  def handle_call({:submit_proposal, proposal}, _from, state) do
    task_id = proposal.task_id
    proposals = Map.update(
      state.proposals, task_id, [proposal],
      &insert_by_priority(&1, proposal)
    )
    new_state = %{state | proposals: proposals}
    {:reply, :ok, maybe_match(new_state, task_id)}
  end

  # Price-time priority insertion
  defp insert_by_priority(existing, new) do
    Enum.sort_by([new | existing], fn p ->
      {p.estimated_credits, p.timestamp}
    end)
  end

  # Match when demand meets supply
  defp maybe_match(state, task_id) do
    with demands when demands != [] <-
           Map.get(state.demands, task_id, []),
         [best | _rest] <-
           Map.get(state.proposals, task_id, []),
         true <- best.estimated_credits <=
                   hd(demands).budget_ceiling do
      execute_match(state, task_id, hd(demands), best)
    else
      _ -> state
    end
  end
end
\end{lstlisting}

The matching engine implements the profunctor action: proposals are indexed by agent and task, and matching selects the optimal element from the fiber $P(-, \tau)$.

\subsection{Escrow GenServer with Mnesia}

The escrow module uses Mnesia for transactional guarantees:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Escrow monad implementation},label={lst:escrow}]
defmodule PlannerEngine.Escrow do
  use GenServer

  # Monad unit: initialize escrow (hold credits)
  @spec hold(String.t(), non_neg_integer(), String.t())
    :: {:ok, String.t()} | {:error, atom()}
  def hold(client_id, amount, contract_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__,
      {:hold, client_id, amount, contract_id})
  end

  # Monad bind: sequence escrow operations
  @spec bind(String.t(), (map() -> {:ok, map()}))
    :: {:ok, map()} | {:error, atom()}
  def bind(escrow_id, f) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__, {:bind, escrow_id, f})
  end

  # Monad join: settle (release or refund)
  @spec settle(String.t(), :release | :refund)
    :: {:ok, map()} | {:error, atom()}
  def settle(escrow_id, action) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__,
      {:settle, escrow_id, action})
  end

  def handle_call({:hold, client_id, amount, cid},
                  _from, state) do
    result = :mnesia.transaction(fn ->
      case :mnesia.read(:balances, client_id) do
        [{:balances, ^client_id, balance}]
          when balance >= amount ->
          :mnesia.write({:balances, client_id,
                         balance - amount})
          escrow_id = generate_id()
          :mnesia.write({:escrows, escrow_id,
            client_id, amount, cid, :held})
          escrow_id
        _ ->
          :mnesia.abort(:insufficient_funds)
      end
    end)

    case result do
      {:atomic, eid} -> {:reply, {:ok, eid}, state}
      {:aborted, reason} ->
        {:reply, {:error, reason}, state}
    end
  end
end
\end{lstlisting}

The Mnesia transaction ensures atomicity: the balance check and deduction happen within a single transaction, preventing the TOCTOU race condition described in \Cref{def:atomic-ops}.

\subsection{Task Decomposition with Supervision Trees}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={DAG-based task decomposition},label={lst:decomposer}]
defmodule PlannerEngine.Decomposer do
  @type subtask :: %{
    id: String.t(),
    parent_task_id: String.t(),
    description: String.t(),
    dependencies: [String.t()],
    status: :pending | :running | :completed | :failed
  }

  @type dag :: %{
    task_id: String.t(),
    subtasks: %{String.t() => subtask()},
    edges: [{String.t(), String.t()}]
  }

  @spec decompose(map()) :: {:ok, dag()} | {:error, term()}
  def decompose(task) do
    subtasks = analyze_and_split(task)
    edges = infer_dependencies(subtasks)

    dag = %{
      task_id: task.id,
      subtasks: Map.new(subtasks, &{&1.id, &1}),
      edges: edges
    }

    case validate_dag(dag) do
      :ok -> {:ok, dag}
      {:error, :cycle} -> {:error, :cyclic_dependency}
    end
  end

  @spec topological_sort(dag()) :: [[String.t()]]
  def topological_sort(dag) do
    in_degrees = compute_in_degrees(dag)

    sources =
      for {id, 0} <- in_degrees, do: id

    build_levels(dag, sources, in_degrees, [])
  end

  defp build_levels(_dag, [], _degrees, levels),
    do: Enum.reverse(levels)
  defp build_levels(dag, current_level, degrees, levels) do
    new_degrees =
      Enum.reduce(current_level, degrees, fn id, acc ->
        dag.edges
        |> Enum.filter(fn {from, _to} -> from == id end)
        |> Enum.reduce(acc, fn {_from, to}, inner_acc ->
          Map.update!(inner_acc, to, &(&1 - 1))
        end)
      end)

    next_level =
      for {id, 0} <- new_degrees,
          id not in List.flatten([current_level | levels]),
          do: id

    build_levels(dag, next_level, new_degrees,
                [current_level | levels])
  end
end
\end{lstlisting}

The \texttt{topological\_sort/1} function implements \Cref{alg:topsort}, returning a list of levels where each level can execute in parallel. The supervision tree spawns a \texttt{Task.Supervisor} for each level, running all subtasks concurrently and awaiting completion before proceeding to the next level.

      {:compute_score, agent_id})
  end

  @spec record_quality(String.t(), [float()]) :: :ok
  def record_quality(agent_id, quality_vector)
    when length(quality_vector) == 6 do
    GenServer.cast(__MODULE__,
      {:record, agent_id, quality_vector})
  end

  @spec trust_score(String.t()) :: float()
  def trust_score(agent_id) do
    GenServer.call(__MODULE__,
      {:trust_score, agent_id})
  end

  def handle_call({:compute_score, agent_id},
                  _from, state) do
    history = Map.get(state.histories, agent_id, [])
    score = exponential_weighted_score(
              history, @default_weights, @decay_factor)
    {:reply, score, state}
  end

  defp exponential_weighted_score([], _w, _l), do: 0.5
  defp exponential_weighted_score(history, weights, lambda) do
    n = length(history)

    {weighted_sum, weight_total} =
      history
      |> Enum.with_index()
      |> Enum.reduce({0.0, 0.0}, fn {qv, i}, {ws, wt} ->
        decay = :math.pow(lambda, n - 1 - i)
        inner = Enum.zip(weights, qv)
                |> Enum.map(fn {w, q} -> w * q end)
                |> Enum.sum()
        {ws + decay * inner, wt + decay}
      end)

    weighted_sum / weight_total
  end
end

\subsection{AI Agent Marketplace: The AgentHero Model}

The AgentHero platform implements the full framework described in this paper. We trace a concrete job through the categorical machinery.

\paragraph{Scenario.} A client needs a web application tested. The job requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Functional testing (Playwright-based E2E tests)
    \item Performance testing (k6 load tests)
    \item Security scanning (OWASP ZAP)
    \item Accessibility audit (axe-core)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Step 1: Task functor.} The client posts the job (status: \texttt{draft}). The task functor $F$ maps it to requirements:
\[
    F(\tau) = (\{\text{playwright}, \text{k6}, \text{zap}, \text{axe}\}, \{t \leq 4\text{h}\}, 5000\text{ credits}, \text{24h deadline})
\]

\paragraph{Step 2: Job decomposition.} The orchestrator applies the decomposition functor $\Delta$:
\[
    \Delta(\tau) = \begin{cases}
        V = \{\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{a11y}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}\} \\
        E = \{(\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{report}}), (\sigma_{\text{a11y}}, \sigma_{\text{report}})\}
    \end{cases}
\]

The topological sort yields levels $L_0 = \{\sigma_{\text{func}}, \sigma_{\text{perf}}, \sigma_{\text{sec}}, \sigma_{\text{a11y}}\}$ (parallel) and $L_1 = \{\sigma_{\text{report}}\}$ (barrier). The critical path length is 2.

\paragraph{Step 3: Order book.} The job moves to \texttt{open}. Three operators submit proposals:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Agent & Credits & Duration & Confidence & Reputation \\
\midrule
$\alpha_1$ (TestBot-Pro) & 3500 & 3h & 0.92 & 0.88 \\
$\alpha_2$ (QA-Agent) & 4200 & 2h & 0.85 & 0.91 \\
$\alpha_3$ (BugHunter) & 2800 & 5h & 0.78 & 0.72 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Step 4: Matching.} The cost functional from \Cref{cor:optimal-matching}:
\begin{align}
    \text{cost}(\alpha_1) &= \frac{3500}{1 + 0.92 \times 0.88} = \frac{3500}{1.8096} \approx 1934 \\
    \text{cost}(\alpha_2) &= \frac{4200}{1 + 0.85 \times 0.91} = \frac{4200}{1.7735} \approx 2368 \\
    \text{cost}(\alpha_3) &= \frac{2800}{1 + 0.78 \times 0.72} = \frac{2800}{1.5616} \approx 1793
\end{align}

$\alpha_3$ has the lowest cost but the lowest confidence and reputation. The client selects $\alpha_1$ (best cost-adjusted quality), triggering:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\alpha_2$ and $\alpha_3$ auto-rejected (colimit universal property);
    \item $\texttt{holdEscrow}(\text{client}, 3500, \text{contract\_42})$ (escrow monad unit);
    \item Contract created, job moves to \texttt{in\_progress}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Step 5: Execution.} The Inngest pipeline executes the DAG. Level $L_0$ runs four subtasks in parallel. Upon completion, barrier synchronization triggers $L_1$ (report generation).

\paragraph{Step 6: Settlement.} The client approves the deliverable. Revenue split:
\begin{align}
    \text{Operator } (\alpha_1) &: 0.70 \times 3500 = 2450 \text{ credits} \\
    \text{Platform} &: 0.15 \times 3500 = 525 \text{ credits} \\
    \text{LLM reserve} &: 0.15 \times 3500 = 525 \text{ credits}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Step 7: Reputation update.} The evaluator scores quality:
\[
    \mathbf{q} = (0.95, 0.90, 0.97, 0.88, 0.91, 0.82)
\]
Agent $\alpha_1$'s reputation is updated via the exponentially-weighted average.

\subsection{Decentralized Task Allocation}

Consider a scenario where no central marketplace exists. Agents discover tasks through a gossip protocol, and matching occurs via distributed consensus. The categorical framework still applies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The order book profunctor $P$ is distributed across nodes, with each node maintaining a local view. Consistency is achieved through CRDTs (conflict-free replicated data types), which form a category with lattice structure.
    \item The escrow monad is implemented via a blockchain or distributed ledger, where the Mnesia transactions are replaced by smart contract calls.
    \item The natural transformation $\eta$ is computed locally by each node and verified globally via the consensus protocol.
\end{itemize}

This demonstrates the framework's generality: the categorical structure is independent of the deployment topology.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Negotiation}

In complex jobs, multiple agents may need to collaborate. The decomposition functor $\Delta$ produces a DAG where different subtasks are matched to different agents. The planner natural transformation $\eta$ must then be a \emph{family} of matchings:
\[
    \eta = \{\eta_{\sigma_i}: F(\sigma_i) \to G(M(\sigma_i))\}_{i=1}^{n}
\]

The naturality condition ensures that agents assigned to dependent subtasks can communicate: if $(\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \in E$, then $M(\sigma_i)$'s output is compatible with $M(\sigma_j)$'s input. This is verified by checking that the profunctor action maps the output type of $G(M(\sigma_i))$ to the input type of $F(\sigma_j)$.

Negotiation between agents for shared resources (e.g., a database connection, a GPU) is modeled as a sub-market within the order book, where the ``tasks'' are resource requests and the ``agents'' are resource providers. The colimit construction applies recursively.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Comparison to Financial Exchanges}

The planner engine's order book bears a structural resemblance to financial exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, CME), but with key differences:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Aspect} & \textbf{Financial Exchange} & \textbf{AI Planner} \\
\midrule
Asset & Fungible (shares, contracts) & Non-fungible (tasks) \\
Pricing & Continuous price discovery & Discrete bidding rounds \\
Settlement & T+1 or T+2 & Upon verified completion \\
Quality & N/A (fungible) & 6-dimensional scoring \\
Counterparty risk & Clearinghouse guarantee & Escrow monad \\
Information & Price, volume & Confidence, reputation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The key innovation is that AI task markets require \emph{quality verification} before settlement, introducing a review phase absent in financial exchanges. The escrow monad handles this by holding funds through the verification period.

\subsection{Comparison to Kubernetes Scheduler}

The Kubernetes scheduler and the AI planner both solve assignment problems, but at different abstraction levels:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Kubernetes:} Assigns deterministic workloads to nodes based on resource requests. The scheduler is centralized and benevolent. Matching is based on hard constraints (predicates) and soft preferences (priorities).
    \item \textbf{AI Planner:} Assigns stochastic workloads to self-interested agents. The planner must incentivize truthful reporting of capabilities. Matching incorporates economic signals (pricing, reputation) in addition to capability constraints.
\end{itemize}

The categorical framework subsumes both: Kubernetes scheduling is the special case where the escrow monad is trivial ($E = \id$) and the reputation functor is constant ($\mathcal{R}(\alpha) = 1$ for all $\alpha$).

\subsection{Mechanism Design Implications}

The revelation principle \cite{myerson1981optimal} suggests that our bidding mechanism can be replaced by a direct revelation mechanism where agents truthfully report their costs and capabilities. The VCG mechanism achieves this by charging each agent the externality they impose on others.

In our framework, the VCG payment for agent $\alpha_k$ winning task $\tau$ is:
\[
    p_k^{\text{VCG}} = \texttt{cost}(\alpha_{k'}) - \sum_{j \neq k} \texttt{cost}(\alpha_j)
\]
where $\alpha_{k'}$ is the second-best agent and the sum represents the total welfare of other agents. This ensures truthful bidding is a dominant strategy.

However, VCG has known vulnerabilities in repeated settings \cite{ausubel2006lovely}: agents can collude across rounds to manipulate prices. Our anti-gaming mechanisms (\Cref{def:gaming}) address this by detecting collusion rings and suspicious patterns.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\paragraph{Bounded rationality.} Our framework assumes agents maximize expected utility. In practice, LLM-based agents may exhibit bounded rationality---they may not perfectly optimize their bids or execution strategies. Behavioral mechanism design \cite{de2014bribery} offers tools for handling such deviations.

\paragraph{Dynamic capabilities.} Agent capabilities evolve over time (through fine-tuning, tool acquisition, or prompt engineering). The capability functor $G$ should be time-dependent: $G_t: \Agents \to \Cap$. The planner natural transformation must then be a natural transformation between time-indexed functors, requiring the framework of \emph{indexed categories}.

\paragraph{Multi-objective optimization.} The cost functional in \Cref{cor:optimal-matching} is a scalar. In practice, clients may have multi-objective preferences (minimize cost, maximize quality, minimize time). Pareto-optimal matching in a multi-criteria setting requires the theory of \emph{enriched profunctors} over partially ordered monoids.

\paragraph{Privacy.} Agents may not wish to reveal their true capabilities or cost structures. Secure multi-party computation (MPC) or zero-knowledge proofs could enable private matching while preserving the colimit structure of market clearing.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a comprehensive categorical framework for the planner engine of an AI operating system, unifying task orchestration, marketplace dynamics, financial mechanics, and reputation management under a single mathematical roof.

The key constructions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textbf{order book profunctor} $P: \Agents^{\op} \times \Tasks \to \Set$, capturing the two-sided market structure of agent proposals and client demands.
    \item The \textbf{planner natural transformation} $\eta: F \Rightarrow G \circ M$, formalizing the matching of task requirements to agent capabilities with provable consistency (naturality).
    \item The \textbf{escrow monad} $(E, \eta, \mu)$ on the credit category, with verified monad laws guaranteeing financial consistency, conservation, and non-negativity.
    \item \textbf{Market clearing as a colimit}, characterizing the acceptance of a proposal and auto-rejection of alternatives via the universal property.
    \item \textbf{Functorial job decomposition} into DAGs with topologically-sorted parallel execution, preserving job semantics.
    \item \textbf{Convergent reputation scoring} via exponentially-weighted 6-dimensional quality vectors with anti-gaming constraints.
\end{enumerate}

The Elixir/OTP implementation demonstrates that these abstractions are not merely theoretical: GenServer order books, Mnesia-backed escrow transactions, and supervision-tree decomposition provide production-ready realizations of the categorical constructs. The AgentHero case study validates the framework against a real system where jobs follow a complete lifecycle from draft to settlement.

The planner engine occupies the highest level of the AI OS abstraction hierarchy. Where the kernel (Part I) provides the type-theoretic foundation, the memory layer (Part II) provides persistent state, and the tool interface (Part III) provides capability extension, the planner engine provides the \emph{economic coordination} that turns a collection of independent agents into a functioning marketplace. Part V will complete the series by presenting the runtime that executes the plans produced by this engine.

The upper path is the planning natural transformation: tasks are mapped to requirements and matched to capabilities. The lower path is the execution pipeline: tasks are decomposed into DAGs, mapped to Inngest durable functions, executed to produce credit transactions, which are processed through the escrow monad and distributed via the revenue split.

Commutativity of the outer rectangle expresses the fundamental correctness property: \emph{planning and then executing is equivalent to decomposing, executing piecewise, and settling financially}.
